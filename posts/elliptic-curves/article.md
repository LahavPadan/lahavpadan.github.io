## § 0. Finite fields: characteristic, order, and the Frobenius exponent

Elliptic curves in cryptography live over finite fields, and Frobenius-based reasoning depends on knowing exactly which power of which prime is being raised to. This section fixes the conventions before the rest of the document uses them.

### The characteristic must be prime

The **characteristic** of a field is the smallest positive integer $n$ such that adding $1$ to itself $n$ times gives $0$:
$$\underbrace{1 + 1 + \cdots + 1}_{n \text{ times}} = 0.$$
(If no such $n$ exists, the characteristic is $0$; that is the case for $\mathbb{Q}, \mathbb{R}, \mathbb{C}$.)

Suppose the characteristic $n$ is composite, $n = ab$ with $1 < a, b < n$. Then
$$0 = n \cdot 1 = (ab) \cdot 1 = (a \cdot 1)(b \cdot 1)$$
by the distributive law. So the product of two nonzero elements $(a \cdot 1)$ and $(b \cdot 1)$ is zero. But a field has no zero divisors: if a product is zero, at least one factor is zero, because otherwise multiplying by the inverse of the nonzero factor would force the other to be zero too — contradicting the minimality of $n$ (since $a, b < n$).

So the characteristic is either $0$ or a prime $p$. Finite fields must have characteristic prime, because the characteristic-$0$ case forces the field to contain a copy of $\mathbb{Q}$, hence infinitely many elements.

### The size must be $p^k$

Fix a finite field $\mathbb{F}$ with characteristic $p$. The set $\{0, 1, 2, \ldots, p-1\}$ generated by repeated addition of $1$ is a subfield; call it $\mathbb{F}_p$. Every element of $\mathbb{F}$ can be added to any other, and any element of $\mathbb{F}$ can be scaled by an element of $\mathbb{F}_p$. Those two operations — internal addition, external scaling by the base field — are precisely the axioms of a **vector space** over $\mathbb{F}_p$.

Since $\mathbb{F}$ is finite, it is a finite-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{F}_p$. Pick a basis $\{v_1, \ldots, v_k\}$. Every element of $\mathbb{F}$ is uniquely
$$c_1 v_1 + c_2 v_2 + \cdots + c_k v_k, \qquad c_i \in \mathbb{F}_p.$$
There are $p$ choices for each $c_i$ and the choices are independent, so $|\mathbb{F}| = p^k$.

Conversely, for every prime power $p^k$ there exists a field of that size (constructed as $\mathbb{F}_p[x]/(f)$ for any irreducible $f \in \mathbb{F}_p[x]$ of degree $k$), unique up to isomorphism. We denote it $\mathbb{F}_{p^k}$ or $\mathbb{F}_q$ with $q = p^k$.

### The Frobenius map: why $x^q$, not $x^p$

In characteristic $p$, expanding $(u + v)^p$ by the binomial theorem gives $\sum_{k=0}^{p} \binom{p}{k} u^k v^{p-k}$, and for $0 < k < p$ the coefficient
$$\binom{p}{k} = \frac{p!}{k!(p-k)!}$$
has a factor of $p$ in the numerator that the denominator cannot cancel (both $k!$ and $(p-k)!$ are products of integers strictly less than the prime $p$, so neither contains $p$). Those middle terms vanish in the field, leaving
$$(u + v)^p = u^p + v^p.$$
Combined with the obvious $(uv)^p = u^p v^p$, the map $x \mapsto x^p$ preserves *both* field operations: sum-of-$p$-th-powers equals $p$-th-power-of-sum, and product-of-$p$-th-powers equals $p$-th-power-of-product. Call this map $\sigma_p$.

**Immediate consequence for curves.** If the coordinates $(x_0, y_0)$ satisfy a polynomial equation like $y^2 = x^3 + a x + b$, then raising both sides to the $p$-th power gives $(y_0^p)^2 = (x_0^p)^3 + a^p x_0^p + b^p$ — but $a, b \in \mathbb{F}_p$ so $a^p = a$ and $b^p = b$ (by the multiplicative-group argument used two paragraphs down, applied to $\mathbb{F}_p$). So $(x_0^p, y_0^p)$ satisfies the *same* equation. The map $\sigma_p$ sends points of the curve to other points of the curve.

**Which exponent for a curve over $\mathbb{F}_q$?** If the curve is defined over an extension field $\mathbb{F}_q$ with $q = p^k$, its coefficients $a, b$ live in $\mathbb{F}_q$, not just $\mathbb{F}_p$. Now $a^p$ generally does *not* equal $a$; what does hold is $a^q = a$. Reason: the nonzero elements of $\mathbb{F}_q$ form a group of size $q - 1$ under multiplication, so any $a \in \mathbb{F}_q^*$ satisfies $a^{q-1} = 1$, hence $a^q = a$; and $0^q = 0$. So the map that leaves the curve equation genuinely fixed — sending points of the curve back to points of the same curve — is $x \mapsto x^q$, not $x \mapsto x^p$. Note that $x^q = ((x^p)^p \cdots)^p$ ($k$ nested applications), so this is just the $p$-power map iterated $k$ times.

**The convention throughout this document.** For a curve $E$ defined over $\mathbb{F}_q$ with $q = p^k$, the **Frobenius** is
$$\pi_q : (x, y) \mapsto (x^q, y^q).$$
The exponent is $q$, not $p$. Every downstream quantity uses $q$:
$$\pi_q^2 - t \pi_q + q = 0, \qquad |t| \leq 2\sqrt{q}, \qquad N = q + 1 - t.$$

When we work over the prime field itself — as with secp256k1, P-256, and Curve25519 — $q = p$ and this distinction collapses; the Frobenius exponent is simply $p$. But whenever an extension field enters (as it does for BLS12-381's target group, or for the $\ell$-torsion of any curve when the $\ell$-torsion doesn't sit inside the base field), the exponent is the full $p^k$.

## § 1. What an elliptic curve actually is

An elliptic curve is a **non-singular cubic** equipped with a distinguished point (the "identity") and a group law derived from intersection with lines. "Cubic" can take many algebraic forms; the most familiar is
$$y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$$
(short Weierstrass form), but this is *one canonical normalization*, not the definition. Other equations describing elliptic curves include the **Fermat cubic** $x^3 + y^3 = 1$ (genuinely a degree-3 curve, just not in $y^2 = f(x)$ form) and the **Edwards form** $x^2 + y^2 = 1 + d x^2 y^2$ (a quartic that's birationally equivalent to a cubic — see § 16 for the explicit substitution that turns it back into Weierstrass). What makes all of them "the same kind of thing" is not the equation shape but the underlying geometric/group structure. The short Weierstrass form is the convenient working form because it's the simplest to manipulate and most theorems are first stated for it.

Three questions immediately deserve answers:

1. Why a *cubic*, specifically?
2. Why *non-singular*, specifically?
3. What is the "point at infinity," really, and where does it come from?

### Why a cubic, and where the group law comes from

The construction we want is: a set with a way to "combine two elements into a third" that satisfies the group axioms (associativity, identity, inverses, commutativity). We could try to invent such a rule from scratch on an arbitrary geometric object, but it would be ad hoc. Instead, we look for a geometric construction that *naturally* produces such a rule.

Start with a curve in the plane. The simplest geometric act on a curve is: draw a line through two of its points, see where the line meets the curve again. If the line always hits the curve in *exactly three places*, then "two points in, one point out" is a well-defined operation. Combined with a choice of identity, this gives a group law — provided we reflect the third intersection across an axis (necessary to make the operation actually invertible).

So the question is: for which curves does a line hit in exactly three places?

For this to work cleanly, a line has to hit the curve in *exactly three places* (counted with the right multiplicity), so that "two points in, one point out" makes sense.

- **Degree 1** (a line): a line hits a line in one place. No third point to play with.
- **Degree 2** (a conic — ellipse, hyperbola, parabola): a line hits a conic in two places. Still no third point.
- **Degree 3** (a cubic): a line hits a cubic in three places. *This is where the construction first becomes possible.*
- Degree 4 and up: a line hits in four-or-more places, and the construction breaks down (which "third" point do you pick?). Higher-degree curves are also genuinely more complicated as group objects — a degree-3 curve gives a torus, a degree-4 generically gives a surface with two holes, etc., and the latter doesn't have a clean addition law on its points.

So the cubic is the minimum complexity at which the geometric group-law trick first works, and it's the maximum complexity at which it remains clean.

### The chord-and-tangent construction

Given the cubic and its three-fold intersection property, here is the actual group law: take two points $P, Q$ on the curve, draw the line through them, find the third intersection $R$, and define $P + Q$ as the reflection of $R$ across the $x$-axis. The reflection step is what makes the operation invertible — without it, you'd get an operation where "P plus Q plus R equals zero," which is fine as a relation but not directly a group law.

The identity element is the point at infinity $\mathcal{O}$ (vertical lines hit the curve at two finite affine points plus this one). The inverse of $(x, y)$ is $(x, -y)$ — the reflection that's already built into the construction.

For doubling ($P + P$): the "line through $P$ and $P$" is the tangent line at $P$. This still hits the curve in two more places, but one of them is $P$ itself with multiplicity 2 (because the tangent touches without crossing), so the third intersection is well-defined.

### Why the line hits exactly three times

This is just the fundamental theorem of algebra in disguise. Substitute the line $y = mx + c$ into the curve $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$:
$$(mx + c)^2 = x^3 + ax + b.$$
This is a cubic in $x$: one $x^3$ term, lower-degree everything else. A cubic polynomial has three roots — possibly all in the base field, possibly some only appearing if you enlarge the field to include certain square roots, but always three counted with multiplicity. Each root gives one intersection point.

(A vertical line $x = x_0$ doesn't fit the form $y = mx + c$. It hits $y^2 = x_0^3 + ax_0 + b$ in two places — at $y = \pm\sqrt{x_0^3 + ax_0 + b}$. To make this still "three intersections," we declare the third intersection to be the **point at infinity**, which lies on every vertical line by convention. We'll see in § 10 that this convention is forced once we work in the right coordinate system.)

### Why non-singular: parameterizing away the curve

A **singular** point on a curve is a place where the curve crosses itself (a *node* — two tangent directions at the same point) or comes to a sharp tip (a *cusp* — only one tangent direction, but the curve "stops" instead of crossing it). At such a point there is no well-defined "third intersection" the way there is for a smooth point.

You can still try to do the group law on the smooth part of a singular cubic (excluding the singular point), and it works — but the resulting group is one of two unsurprising things:

**Cusp case** ($y^2 = x^3$): introduce the parameter $t = y/x$ on the smooth part. From the equation, $t^2 = y^2/x^2 = x^3/x^2 = x$, so $x = t^2$ and then $y = tx = t^3$. The map $t \mapsto (t^2, t^3)$ takes the additive line $\mathbb{F}$ (with $t = 0$ corresponding to the singular point) onto the curve, bijectively on the smooth part. Negation on the curve is $(x, y) \mapsto (x, -y)$, which corresponds to $t \mapsto -t$. To see the group law in the $t$-parameter: a line $y = mx + c$ hits the curve where $(mx + c)^2 = x^3$, i.e., at the roots of a cubic in $x$. Substituting $x = t^2$, $y = t^3$, this becomes a cubic in $t$. By Vieta's formulas, the three roots $t_1, t_2, t_3$ of that cubic sum to a value determined by the line's coefficients, and chasing through, the chord-and-tangent law on the curve translates into $t_1 + t_2 + t_3 = 0$ in the parameter — i.e., **the group law in $t$ is ordinary addition**. The smooth part of $y^2 = x^3$ is isomorphic, as a group, to $(\mathbb{F}, +)$.

**Node case** ($y^2 = x^2(x + 1)$, with the node at the origin): the curve factors near the singular point as $y \approx \pm x \sqrt{1 + x}$, two branches crossing. Set $t = (y + x \sqrt{1+x}) / (y - x\sqrt{1+x})$ or, more cleanly: parameterize by $t \mapsto \left(\frac{(1-t)^2}{4t}, \frac{(1-t)^2(1+t)}{8t \sqrt{t}}\right)$ (or any similar rational parameterization picking out a coordinate on the smooth branch). After the dust settles, the chord-and-tangent law becomes $t_1 \cdot t_2 = t_3$ in this parameter — i.e., **the group law is ordinary multiplication**. The smooth part is isomorphic to the multiplicative group $(\mathbb{F}^*, \cdot)$.

In either case, the "group" of a singular cubic is something we already know how to do discrete logarithms in: division is trivial for the additive group, and index-calculus methods give sub-exponential discrete log for the multiplicative group. So singular cubics are cryptographically useless.

> **Visual sanity check.** A *node* looks like an X — two branches crossing transversally, with two distinct tangent lines at the crossing. A *cusp* looks like a comma — the curve comes in, makes a sharp turn, and leaves, with only one tangent direction (a horizontal tangent for $y^2 = x^3$ at the origin). A *smooth* elliptic curve over $\mathbb{R}$ looks like either one connected egg-shaped loop, or two pieces — a loop plus an unbounded curve — depending on whether the right-hand-side cubic has one or three real roots.

The intuitive picture, restated: the smoothness condition is precisely what stops the curve from being globally parameterizable by a single coordinate $t$. The presence of a node or cusp creates a "natural coordinate" $t = y/x$ that unrolls the curve, and once unrolled, the group structure is forced to be the boring additive or multiplicative one. A smooth cubic has no such global $t$ — locally near any point you can use $x$ as a coordinate, but you can't extend that to the whole curve without redundancy or breaks. That topological obstruction is exactly what makes the group on a smooth elliptic curve genuinely new structure, not reducible to addition or multiplication in a field.

## § 2. The singular point: derivation of the discriminant

We want a clean algebraic test for singularity. A singular point $P = (x_0, y_0)$ on $F(x, y) = y^2 - x^3 - ax - b = 0$ is defined by three simultaneous conditions: $F = 0$, $\partial F/\partial x = 0$, $\partial F/\partial y = 0$.

Computing the partials:
$$\frac{\partial F}{\partial y} = 2y, \qquad \frac{\partial F}{\partial x} = -3x^2 - a.$$

Vanishing of $\partial F/\partial y$ forces $y_0 = 0$ (assuming $2 \neq 0$ in the field). Vanishing of $\partial F/\partial x$ forces $3x_0^2 + a = 0$. Combined with $F(x_0, 0) = 0$, we have three constraints:
$$y_0 = 0, \qquad 3x_0^2 + a = 0, \qquad x_0^3 + ax_0 + b = 0.$$

These three equations in two unknowns $(x_0, a, b)$ — well, in $x_0$, with $a, b$ as parameters of the curve — are typically overdetermined. They have a solution if and only if the parameters $a, b$ satisfy a single algebraic condition. To find it, eliminate $x_0$.

From the middle equation: $x_0^2 = -a/3$. Multiply the curve equation $x_0^3 + ax_0 + b = 0$ by $x_0$: $x_0^4 + ax_0^2 + bx_0 = 0$, hence $(x_0^2)^2 + a x_0^2 + b x_0 = 0$. Substitute $x_0^2 = -a/3$:
$$\frac{a^2}{9} - \frac{a^2}{3} + bx_0 = 0 \implies bx_0 = \frac{2a^2}{9} \implies x_0 = \frac{2a^2}{9b}.$$

Now demand consistency: square the result and equate with the earlier expression for $x_0^2$:
$$\left(\frac{2a^2}{9b}\right)^2 = -\frac{a}{3} \implies \frac{4 a^4}{81 b^2} = -\frac{a}{3} \implies 12 a^4 = -81 a b^2 \implies 4 a^3 = -27 b^2$$
(dividing by $3a$, assuming $a \neq 0$; the $a = 0$ case is handled separately and gives $b = 0$, consistent).

So the curve has a singular point **if and only if** $4a^3 + 27 b^2 = 0$.

### Connection to the cubic's classical discriminant

If you've seen the **discriminant of a depressed cubic** $p(x) = x^3 + ax + b$ — the quantity that vanishes precisely when $p$ has a repeated root — it's $-(4a^3 + 27b^2)$. Up to sign, exactly what we just derived.

This is no coincidence. The curve $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$ has a singular point with $y_0 = 0$ exactly when $x^3 + ax + b$ has a repeated root, because:
- $y_0 = 0$ forces $x_0$ to be a root of $f(x) = x^3 + ax + b$.
- $\partial F/\partial x = 0$ at $y_0 = 0$ forces $x_0$ to also be a root of $f'(x) = 3x^2 + a$.
- A number that's a root of both $f$ and $f'$ is a **repeated** root of $f$.

So singularity of the curve happens precisely when the right-hand-side cubic has a double root. The discriminant of the curve and the discriminant of the cubic are detecting the same phenomenon.

### Cusp vs. node, geometrically

When $f(x) = x^3 + ax + b$ has a repeated root, two sub-cases:

- **Double root plus simple root** (a triple-root degeneration: two of three roots collide, one stays apart). Near the repeated root $x_0$, $f(x) \approx (x - x_0)^2 (x - x_1)$, so $y^2 \approx (x - x_0)^2 (x - x_1)$, hence $y \approx \pm (x - x_0)\sqrt{x - x_1}$. Two distinct branches crossing at $(x_0, 0)$: a **node**. Two tangent directions.

- **Triple root** (all three roots coincide). $f(x) = (x - x_0)^3$, so $y^2 = (x - x_0)^3$, hence near $(x_0, 0)$ the curve obeys $y \sim (x - x_0)^{3/2}$. One tangent direction (horizontal: $y' = 0$), with the curve coming in and going out along it, forming a sharp tip: a **cusp**.

The discriminant detects "repeated root somewhere"; whether the resulting singularity is a node or a cusp is determined by the multiplicity. The cusp case is the rarer degeneration ($a = b = 0$ gives the standard cusp $y^2 = x^3$); the node case is generic among singular curves.

### Where the standard $-16$ normalization comes from

Convention writes the discriminant as $\Delta = -16(4a^3 + 27 b^2)$, not just $4a^3 + 27 b^2$. The $-16$ has an organic origin, not a conventional one.

Redo the singularity elimination for the *general* Weierstrass equation (before setting $a_1 = a_2 = a_3 = 0$). The cubic condition that comes out is $4x^3 + b_2 x^2 + 2 b_4 x + b_6 = 0$, with $b_2 = a_1^2 + 4a_2$, $b_4 = 2 a_4 + a_1 a_3$, $b_6 = a_3^2 + 4 a_6$. Specializing back to the short case makes it collapse to $4(x^3 + a x + b) = 4 f(x)$, not $f(x)$ — because $y^2 = f(x)$ gives $(2y)^2 = 4 f(x)$, and $2y$ is exactly the quantity we divide by in the tangent slope $\lambda = f'(x)/(2y)$: the same "$/2$" from completing the square, squared once we look at $F = 0$ (a squared quantity, $y^2$) rather than a slope. Taking the standard discriminant of $C(x) = 4 f(x)$ (resultant of $C, C'$ divided by the leading coefficient) gives $-256 (4 a^3 + 27 b^2)$; the factor $256 = 4^4$ falls out mechanically because scaling a cubic and its derivative by $k$ scales the resultant by $k^5$, and dividing by the leading coefficient removes one power of $k$.

The final $/16$ giving $\Delta = -16(4 a^3 + 27 b^2)$ is the one genuinely conventional choice — and it is picked so that $\Delta$, together with two companion invariants $c_4 = -48 a$ and $c_6 = -864 b$ (built from the same elimination, tracking different combinations of the $b_i$), satisfy one parameter-free identity:
$$1728 \, \Delta = c_4^3 - c_6^2.$$
Without the $/16$, this would carry a factor of $27648$ instead of $1728 = 12^3$. The $/16$ minimizes the constant. The invariants $c_4, c_6, \Delta$ return in § 15A: over $\mathbb{C}$ they are the natural invariants of an elliptic curve viewed as a torus, and the identity $1728\Delta = c_4^3 - c_6^2$ is the algebraic shadow of a geometric relationship on lattices.

## § 4. Reduction to short Weierstrass form

The general Weierstrass equation
$$y^2 + a_1 xy + a_3 y = x^3 + a_2 x^2 + a_4 x + a_6$$
has five coefficients ($a_5$ is missing only as a numbering quirk; there is no $x^{1.5}$-style term being suppressed). Two coordinate moves reduce this to the simpler $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$, *provided* we're not in characteristic 2 or 3.

The two moves are both classical: complete the square in $y$, then shift $x$ to kill the quadratic term in the cubic. Each move requires a division — by 2 for the square, by 3 for the cube — which is exactly why the characteristic restriction shows up.

### Killing the linear-in-$y$ terms (needs $2 \neq 0$)

The left side is $y^2 + (a_1 x + a_3) y$, a quadratic in $y$ with linear coefficient $B = a_1 x + a_3$. The completion of the square is the substitution
$$Y = y + \tfrac{B}{2} = y + \tfrac{a_1 x + a_3}{2},$$
which transforms $y^2 + By$ into $Y^2 - \tfrac{B^2}{4}$. Moving the $-B^2/4$ to the right side absorbs it into the cubic in $x$:
$$Y^2 = x^3 + a_2 x^2 + a_4 x + a_6 + \tfrac{(a_1 x + a_3)^2}{4}.$$

The right side is now purely a cubic in $x$ (with new coefficients), the left is just $Y^2$. The step is undefined if $2 = 0$: in characteristic 2, you cannot halve the $a_1 x + a_3$ term, and the form $y^2 + a_1 xy + a_3 y$ is genuinely fundamental and irreducible.

### Killing the $x^2$ term in the cubic (needs $3 \neq 0$)

The cubic is now $x^3 + c x^2 + dx + e$ for some new coefficients $c, d, e$. Shift the $x$ variable to absorb $c$: substitute
$$X = x + \tfrac{c}{3}.$$
The cube term expands as $X^3 - cX^2 + \tfrac{c^2}{3} X - \tfrac{c^3}{27}$, and the $-cX^2$ from the cube cancels the $+cX^2$ from $c \cdot (X - c/3)^2$. Result: $X^3 + \text{(linear in } X\text{)} + \text{(constant)}$, no $X^2$. The step is undefined in characteristic 3.

Calling the resulting coefficients $a$ and $b$ gives us the short Weierstrass form
$$Y^2 = X^3 + a X + b.$$

**Geometric interpretation of each move.** The first move (complete the square in $y$) is just shifting the curve vertically so it's centered on the $x$-axis — making the $y \to -y$ symmetry manifest. The second move (shift $x$) is centering the curve horizontally at the *inflection point* of the cubic $f(x) = x^3 + ax + b$, which is at $x = 0$ for the short form (since $f''(0) = 0$ when $f$ has no $x^2$ term). In characteristic 2, "$y$-axis symmetry" works differently (because $-y = y$ everywhere); in characteristic 3, the cubic's inflection sits "at infinity" and centering on it fails. These aren't conveniences we lose — the underlying geometry of the curve genuinely changes in those characteristics.

## § 5. Counting points and the trace of Frobenius

We work over the finite field $\mathbb{F}_p$ with $p$ elements. We want to count the number of solutions $(x, y) \in \mathbb{F}_p^2$ to $y^2 = f(x) = x^3 + ax + b$, plus the point at infinity.

### Counting $y$ values for each $x$

Fix an $x \in \mathbb{F}_p$. The equation $y^2 = f(x)$ has:
- **two** solutions if $f(x)$ is a nonzero square;
- **one** solution ($y = 0$) if $f(x) = 0$;
- **zero** solutions if $f(x)$ is a non-square.

This is just counting square roots in $\mathbb{F}_p$, no curve theory involved.

### The Legendre symbol

For nonzero $a \in \mathbb{F}_p$ the **Legendre symbol** $\left(\frac{a}{p}\right)$ is defined to be $+1$ if $a$ is a nonzero square in $\mathbb{F}_p$, and $-1$ if not. We extend it by $\left(\frac{0}{p}\right) = 0$.

With this notation, the number of $y$-solutions for a given $x$ is exactly
$$1 + \left(\frac{f(x)}{p}\right).$$

Check: if $f(x)$ is a nonzero square, $1 + 1 = 2$. If $f(x) = 0$, $1 + 0 = 1$. If $f(x)$ is a non-square, $1 + (-1) = 0$. Perfect.

### Why the Legendre symbol equals $a^{(p-1)/2} \pmod p$

This isn't used until § 6, but the fact is short and worth stating where the Legendre symbol first appears.

The nonzero elements of $\mathbb{F}_p$ form a group of size $p - 1$ under multiplication. Any element of a finite group of size $n$ raised to the $n$-th power gives the identity (write the element $a$ and look at $a, a^2, \ldots$ until it cycles back; the cycle length $d$ divides $n$, and $a^n = (a^d)^{n/d} = 1$). So $a^{p-1} = 1$ for every $a \neq 0$. Then $a^{(p-1)/2}$ is a square root of $1$, which can only be $\pm 1$.

Now: $a$ is a square iff $a = g^{2k}$ for some $k$, where $g$ is a generator of $\mathbb{F}_p^*$. Then $a^{(p-1)/2} = g^{k(p-1)} = 1$. Conversely, if $a = g^{2k+1}$ (a non-square), $a^{(p-1)/2} = g^{(2k+1)(p-1)/2} = g^{(p-1)/2} \cdot g^{k(p-1)} = g^{(p-1)/2}$. Since $g$ generates the group, $g^{(p-1)/2}$ is the unique element of order 2, which is $-1$.

So $a^{(p-1)/2} \equiv \left(\frac{a}{p}\right) \pmod p$. (This is **Euler's criterion**, and we'll use it for the Hasse invariant.)

### The point count

Summing over all $x$ and adding 1 for the point at infinity:
$$N = 1 + \sum_{x \in \mathbb{F}_p} \left(1 + \left(\frac{f(x)}{p}\right)\right) = 1 + p + \sum_{x \in \mathbb{F}_p} \left(\frac{f(x)}{p}\right).$$

Define the **trace of Frobenius** $t$ by
$$t = -\sum_{x \in \mathbb{F}_p} \left(\frac{f(x)}{p}\right).$$

Then $N = p + 1 - t$.

### What "trace" really refers to

At this stage $t$ is just a sum, named "trace of Frobenius" by stipulation. The actual reason it deserves the name "trace" comes from § 13–14: there is a specific endomorphism of the curve, the Frobenius map $\pi: (x, y) \to (x^q, y^q)$, which acts as a $2 \times 2$ matrix $M_\pi$ on the curve's $\ell$-torsion (for any prime $\ell \neq p$). The trace of *that matrix* — meaning $\text{tr}(M_\pi)$ in the standard linear-algebra sense — turns out to equal exactly the integer $t = q + 1 - N$ defined above. So the name is forced by an equality we'll prove later, not by any informal analogy now.

## § 6. The Hasse invariant: detecting supersingularity from coefficients alone

A curve over $\mathbb{F}_p$ is called **supersingular** when its trace $t \equiv 0 \pmod{p}$. We'll see in § 14 why this is a meaningful definition; for now, we want to compute it from the curve's coefficients without iterating over all $p$ values of $x$.

### Setup

By Euler's criterion, $\left(\frac{f(x)}{p}\right) \equiv f(x)^{(p-1)/2} \pmod p$ for $f(x) \neq 0$. (When $f(x) = 0$, both sides are $0$.) So
$$-t \equiv \sum_{x \in \mathbb{F}_p} f(x)^{(p-1)/2} \pmod p.$$

Let $H_p(x) = f(x)^{(p-1)/2}$. This is a polynomial in $x$ of degree $3(p-1)/2$ (since $f$ has degree 3). Write it as
$$H_p(x) = \sum_k c_k x^k.$$

> [!info] A lemma on power sums
>
> We need to know $\sum_{x \in \mathbb{F}_p} x^k$ for each $k$. The claim is:
$$\sum_{x \in \mathbb{F}_p} x^k = \begin{cases} -1 & \text{if } (p-1) \mid k \text{ and } k > 0, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise}. \end{cases}$$
**Proof:** Let $g$ be a generator of $\mathbb{F}_p^*$. As $x$ ranges over $\mathbb{F}_p^*$, $x^k$ takes values $1, g^k, g^{2k}, \ldots, g^{(p-2)k}$. So $$\sum_{x \in \mathbb{F}_p^*} x^k = \sum_{j=0}^{p-2} (g^k)^j.$$
If $g^k = 1$ (i.e., $(p-1) \mid k$), this sum is $p-1 \equiv -1 \pmod p$. Otherwise, it's a geometric series with ratio $g^k \neq 1$:
$$\sum_{j=0}^{p-2} (g^k)^j = \frac{(g^k)^{p-1} - 1}{g^k - 1} = \frac{1 - 1}{g^k - 1} = 0.$$
Add the $x = 0$ contribution: $0^k = 0$ for $k > 0$, and $0^0 = 1$. So including $x = 0$ shifts the $k = 0$ case from $p - 1 \equiv -1$ to $p \equiv 0$, but for $k > 0$ nothing changes. The lemma is proved.

###### Collapsing the sum
$$\sum_{x \in \mathbb{F}_p} H_p(x) = \sum_k c_k \sum_x x^k.$$
The inner sum is zero except when $k$ is a positive multiple of $p - 1$. Since $\deg H_p = 3(p-1)/2 < 2(p-1)$, the only relevant $k$ in our polynomial is $k = p - 1$. So
$$\sum_{x \in \mathbb{F}_p} H_p(x) \equiv -c_{p-1} \pmod p,$$
where $c_{p-1}$ is the coefficient of $x^{p-1}$ in $(x^3 + ax + b)^{(p-1)/2}$.
###### Conclusion

Putting it all together, $t \equiv c_{p-1} \pmod p$, and the curve is supersingular precisely when $c_{p-1} \equiv 0 \pmod p$. The number $c_{p-1}$ is called the **Hasse invariant** of the curve.

> **The Hasse invariant is not a black-box gadget.** It is literally the coefficient of one specific power of $x$ in one specific polynomial that you can compute by hand for small $p$. The curve $y^2 = x^3 + 1$ over $\mathbb{F}_5$: compute $(x^3 + 1)^2 = x^6 + 2x^3 + 1$, look at the coefficient of $x^{p-1} = x^4$ — it's $0$ — and conclude the curve is supersingular over $\mathbb{F}_5$. No theory required.

## § 7. The quadratic twist

Given an elliptic curve $E: y^2 = f(x)$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$ and a non-square $d \in \mathbb{F}_p^*$, the **quadratic twist** is
$$E^d: \quad d y^2 = f(x).$$

### Why this is "morally the same curve"

If $d$ had a square root in $\mathbb{F}_p$, we could substitute $Y = y \sqrt{d}$, turning $d y^2 = f(x)$ into $Y^2 = f(x)$ — the original curve. So over any field where $\sqrt{d}$ exists, $E$ and $E^d$ are isomorphic.

But by assumption $d$ has no square root in $\mathbb{F}_p$. So the substitution is unavailable, and over $\mathbb{F}_p$, $E$ and $E^d$ are *different* curves — different point sets, different point counts. They become identical only once you enlarge the field to contain $\sqrt{d}$ — which means moving to $\mathbb{F}_{p^2}$.

### Counting points on the twist

For each $x \in \mathbb{F}_p$, the equation $d y^2 = f(x)$ has the same number of solutions as $y^2 = d^{-1} f(x)$ — they're equivalent equations. The number of $y$ satisfying this is
$$1 + \left(\frac{d^{-1} f(x)}{p}\right) = 1 + \left(\frac{d^{-1}}{p}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{f(x)}{p}\right).$$

Now $\left(\frac{d^{-1}}{p}\right) = \left(\frac{d}{p}\right)^{-1} = (-1)^{-1} = -1$, since $d$ is a non-square. So the count is $1 - \left(\frac{f(x)}{p}\right)$. Summing and adding 1 for infinity:
$$N' = 1 + \sum_x \left(1 - \left(\frac{f(x)}{p}\right)\right) = p + 1 + t.$$

### The conservation law

$$N + N' = (p + 1 - t) + (p + 1 + t) = 2p + 2.$$

Two corollaries fall out:
- The sum is fixed by $p$ alone — no dependence on the curve coefficients $a, b$.
- The traces are negatives of each other: $t' = -t$.

This is genuinely useful in practice: knowing the order of one curve immediately tells you the order of its twist. If $N = p + 1 - t$, then $N' = 2p + 2 - N$, no work required.

### Twist security in practice

In protocols like X25519, the user only sends an $x$-coordinate; the server computes assuming that $x$ corresponds to a point on the curve, but if the attacker chooses $x$ such that $f(x)$ is a non-square, the implied point actually lives on the twist. If the attacker can do this, they get to perform discrete-log-style attacks in whichever group is *smaller* — the curve's or the twist's. A curve is **twist-secure** when both the curve and its twist have group orders that are (or contain) a very large prime, leaving no small subgroup for the attacker to attack.

Curve25519: the curve order is $8 \cdot \ell$ where $\ell$ is a 252-bit prime. By the conservation law, the twist order is $2p + 2 - 8\ell$; computing this shows it's $4 \cdot \ell'$ where $\ell'$ is also a huge prime. So Curve25519 is twist-secure: there is no small subgroup attack to exploit on either side. (The factor of 8 in the curve and 4 in the twist together account for "$8 \ell + 4 \ell' = 2p + 2$," which you can check.)

## § 8. Maps between curves: from group structure to rational functions

### The hierarchy of map types

Let $E_1, E_2$ be elliptic curves. A **homomorphism** is a map $\phi: E_1 \to E_2$ that respects the group law: $\phi(P + Q) = \phi(P) + \phi(Q)$. Specializing:

- **Endomorphism**: a homomorphism $E \to E$ (same curve as source and target).
- **Isomorphism**: a homomorphism with a two-sided inverse (bijection that preserves structure).
- **Automorphism**: an isomorphism $E \to E$.
- **Isogeny**: a non-zero homomorphism with finite kernel.

(*Non-zero* means: not the map sending every point to the identity $\mathcal{O}$. *Finite kernel* means: only finitely many points $P$ satisfy $\phi(P) = \mathcal{O}$.)

The collection of all endomorphisms forms a ring $\text{End}(E)$ under pointwise addition (using the group law on $E$) and composition for multiplication. The unit element is the identity map, the zero element is the constant-$\mathcal{O}$ map.

> An endomorphism that happens to be non-zero with finite kernel *is* an isogeny. So Frobenius and multiplication-by-$n$ are both endomorphisms (because source = target) and isogenies (because non-zero, finite kernel). When we say "the isogeny $[n]$" or "the endomorphism $[n]$," we're emphasizing different aspects of the same map.

### The maps we'll actually use throughout

Before the rational-function story, it helps to fix names on the maps that appear over and over:

- **Multiplication-by-$n$**, written $[n]$: the map $P \mapsto P + P + \cdots + P$ ($n$ times). This is an endomorphism. Its kernel — points $P$ with $nP = \mathcal{O}$ — is denoted $E[n]$ and called the **$n$-torsion**.
- **Frobenius**, $\pi: (x, y) \mapsto (x^q, y^q)$, on a curve defined over $\mathbb{F}_q$. Endomorphism, inseparable, degree $q$. Its fixed points are the $\mathbb{F}_q$-rational points.
- **Vélu isogenies**: given a finite subgroup $K$ of $E$, there is an explicit isogeny $\phi: E \to E/K$ whose kernel is $K$. The construction (Vélu's formulas) writes down $\phi$ as a rational function with denominator $\prod_{Q \in K \setminus \mathcal{O}} (x - x_Q)$. These are the basic building blocks of isogeny-based cryptography (§ 22).
- **Complex multiplication endomorphisms**: for special curves with extra symmetries, there are endomorphisms beyond $[n]$. For example, on $y^2 = x^3 + ax$ (with $a$ chosen appropriately) the map $(x, y) \mapsto (-x, iy)$ (where $i^2 = -1$) is an endomorphism. The endomorphism ring is then bigger than $\mathbb{Z}$ — it contains $i$. Such curves have **complex multiplication** (CM).

### Why "torsion" is called that

The word "torsion" is borrowed from module theory: in an abelian group, an element is called a *torsion element* if some positive integer multiple of it is the identity. The set of $n$-torsion points $E[n]$ is everything killed by the integer $n$. There's no mechanical or physical "twisting" meaning — the word descends from the general language for "elements with finite order."

### Why a homomorphism between elliptic curves must be expressible as rational functions

Two facts make this forced. The curve points are described by a polynomial equation in $(x, y)$, and the chord-and-tangent group law produces $P + Q$ via explicit rational formulas in the coordinates of $P$ and $Q$ (slope $\lambda$ from quotient of differences, new $x$ from $\lambda^2 - 2x$, etc.). A homomorphism $\phi: E_1 \to E_2$ must commute with these rational formulas.

**Step A**: $\phi(x, y) = (X, Y)$ for some functions $X, Y$ of $(x, y)$. The negation rule $\phi(-P) = -\phi(P)$ — which follows from $\phi(\mathcal{O}) = \mathcal{O}$ and $\phi(P) + \phi(-P) = \phi(P + (-P)) = \mathcal{O}$ — combined with $-P = (x, -y)$ and $-\phi(P) = (X, -Y)$ forces $X(x, -y) = X(x, y)$ and $Y(x, -y) = -Y(x, y)$.

So $X$ is even in $y$ and $Y$ is odd in $y$. On the curve, $y^2 = f(x)$, so any even polynomial in $y$ collapses to a polynomial in $x$, and any odd polynomial is $y$ times a polynomial in $x$. Allowing division (denominators are needed because the group law has $1/y$ in slopes): $X = R_1(x)$ and $Y = y \cdot R_2(x)$ for rational functions $R_1, R_2$.

**Step B (why rational and not just even/odd of arbitrary type)**: $\phi$ must satisfy $\phi(P + Q) = \phi(P) + \phi(Q)$ as functional identities, for all $P, Q$ on the curve. Both sides are built from polynomial operations in the coordinates of $P, Q$ — the LHS plugs $(P + Q)$'s coordinates (themselves rational in $P, Q$) into $R_1, R_2$; the RHS feeds $\phi(P), \phi(Q)$ through the rational group law. Suppose $R_1$ were not rational — say, contained $\sqrt[3]{x}$ or $\exp(x)$ or similar. Then composing $R_1$ with the rational group-law output of $P + Q$ would produce expressions involving roots or transcendentals of polynomial-in-$x_P, x_Q$ quantities. The RHS, however, only ever produces polynomial-and-quotient expressions in $x_P, y_P, x_Q, y_Q$. Equating "polynomial-and-quotient" with "expression involving $\sqrt[3]{}$ or $\exp$" can only hold identically if the non-rational pieces don't actually appear — i.e., if $R_1$ was already rational.

That's the structural reason. The compatibility forced by the rational group law propagates back to force $R_1, R_2$ rational. No appeal to "varieties" or "morphisms" — just the algebraic fact that "rational-function-of-rational-function = rational function" and the homomorphism condition tying both sides together.

### Why an isogeny is surjective

We've established $\phi(x, y) = (R_1(x), y R_2(x))$ for rational functions $R_i$. To show $\phi$ hits every point on $E_2$ (over a large enough field), we need: for any target $(X_0, Y_0)$ on $E_2$, there exists $(x, y)$ on $E_1$ with $R_1(x) = X_0$ and $y R_2(x) = Y_0$.

Setting up the first equation: $R_1(x) = X_0$ means $\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)} = X_0$, i.e., $P(x) - X_0 \cdot Q(x) = 0$. This is a polynomial equation in $x$. **A polynomial equation always has a root**, provided we allow the field to be large enough to contain the roots — by the fundamental theorem of algebra, every nonconstant polynomial of degree $n$ has exactly $n$ roots in some big enough extension field.

So we can solve for $x$. Then $y^2 = f(x)$ determines $y$ up to sign, again possibly requiring a field extension. Then $y R_2(x) = Y_0$ is consistent because $\phi$ is well-defined as a map between curves (the curve equation on $E_2$ is automatically satisfied: $(y R_2(x))^2 = y^2 R_2(x)^2 = f(x) R_2(x)^2$, and on $E_2$ we need $Y^2 = X^3 + \ldots$, which is satisfied because $\phi$ was constructed to be a curve-to-curve map).

The single phrase "over a large enough field" is doing real work: the points of $E_1$ mapping to a generic point of $E_2$ may live in a field strictly larger than our base $\mathbb{F}_p$. This is a real limitation if you wanted to literally find preimages in $\mathbb{F}_p$. But as a statement of *what the map does*, surjectivity is just the fundamental theorem of algebra applied to $P(x) - X_0 \cdot Q(x)$.

### The non-zero case
If $R_1$ is a constant, then $\phi$ sends every point to the same $x$-coordinate. Then $y R_2(x)$ would have to also be constant, which forces $R_2 = 0$ (or the image is just a single point on the curve, only consistent if that point has $y = 0$ — but then for every input, the output has $y = 0$, and the image is a finite set, so $\phi$ is the constant map sending everything to that point). The only constant map that's a homomorphism is the one sending everything to $\mathcal{O}$. So a *non-constant* homomorphism has non-constant $R_1$, and the above argument gives surjectivity.

So an isogeny is exactly: a homomorphism whose $x$-coordinate map $R_1$ is non-constant. Surjectivity follows from the fundamental theorem of algebra, not from anything fancier.

## § 9. The degree of an isogeny: what it actually counts

What does $\deg(\phi) = \max(\deg P, \deg Q)$ actually mean? Why the max?

### Counting preimages

We just saw that for a generic target $X_0$ on $E_2$, the preimages on $E_1$ are solutions to $P(x) - X_0 Q(x) = 0$. This is a polynomial of degree $\max(\deg P, \deg Q)$ — the highest-degree term is either $P$'s leading term or $X_0 Q$'s leading term, whichever has higher degree.

(If $\deg P > \deg Q$, the polynomial has leading term coming from $P$. If $\deg P < \deg Q$, from $X_0 Q$. If $\deg P = \deg Q$, the polynomial has leading term $(\text{leading coef of } P - X_0 \cdot \text{leading coef of } Q) \cdot x^{\deg P}$, which is degree $\deg P$ for generic $X_0$.)

So: **a generic target has $\max(\deg P, \deg Q)$ preimages** (counted with multiplicity), in some sufficiently extended field. That's what we *call* the degree of $\phi$.

The maximum, not the sum, because the degree of $P(x) - X_0 Q(x)$ is the max of the degrees of its summands. This is a property of polynomial degrees, not a definition we chose.

### Composition multiplies degrees

If $\phi$ has degree $m$ (a generic point has $m$ preimages) and $\psi$ has degree $n$, then $\psi \circ \phi$ has degree $mn$: each preimage in the intermediate curve has $m$ further preimages back in the source.

This means: $\deg: \text{End}(E) \to \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ is **multiplicative** under composition. We use this property repeatedly later.

### The kernel: where preimages collide

The **kernel** $\ker(\phi)$ is the set of points $P$ with $\phi(P) = \mathcal{O}$ — the preimage of the identity in $E_2$.

For a generic target, $\phi$ has $\deg(\phi)$ preimages. For the special target $\mathcal{O}$, the number of preimages is the size of the kernel, $|\ker(\phi)|$.

Now: how do these relate?

If preimages always come in tidy groups of $\deg(\phi)$ distinct points — no preimages ever coincide — then in particular $|\ker(\phi)| = \deg(\phi)$. This is the case for what we'll call **separable** isogenies.

If preimages can stack on top of each other (multiple preimages collapsing to the same point), then $|\ker(\phi)|$ can be strictly less than $\deg(\phi)$. This is the case for **inseparable** isogenies.

The "$\max(\deg P, \deg Q)$" number always counts with multiplicity. So degrees count "algebraic preimages" while kernel size counts "distinct preimages."

> **Is "degree" specific to isogenies?** No. It's the same notion of degree as for any rational function $\mathbb{F}_p \to \mathbb{F}_p$ given by $P(x)/Q(x)$: a generic value has $\max(\deg P, \deg Q)$ preimages, and the degree is multiplicative under composition. The elliptic-curve version is just this notion lifted to two-variable rational maps that happen to respect a group law.

### The kernel size influences the rational functions

The kernel is the set of $P$ with $\phi(P) = \mathcal{O}$, which means $R_1(P_x) = \infty$ (the $x$-coordinate of the identity, in projective terms). For $R_1(x) = P(x)/Q(x)$ to "go to infinity," the denominator $Q(x)$ must vanish. So the $x$-coordinates of the kernel points are exactly the roots of $Q(x)$.

Concretely: if $\ker(\phi)$ contains points $\pm P_1, \pm P_2, \ldots, \pm P_k$ with $x$-coordinates $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_k$ (kernel points come in $\pm$ pairs except for points of order 2 and the identity), then
$$Q(x) = \prod_{i=1}^{k} (x - \alpha_i),$$
modulo a constant. This is **Vélu's formula** in disguise (referenced in the original document under Montgomery curves): the denominator of the isogeny's $x$-coordinate map factors as the product of $(x - \alpha)$ over kernel $x$-coordinates.

The degree of $Q$ is thus controlled by the kernel size: $\deg Q = |\ker(\phi)| - 1$ (subtracting 1 for the point at infinity, which is always in the kernel and contributes a "$1/Q$ blowing up at infinity" effect rather than a factor). The numerator $P$ then has degree one more than $Q$ in the separable case, making the max equal to the kernel size — confirming $\deg \phi = |\ker \phi|$ for separable maps.

## § 10. Separability: what the derivative actually detects

We need to understand: why does the formal derivative test ($R_1'(x) = 0$) tell us whether the kernel size equals the degree?

### The intuition: distinguishable preimages

Consider an ordinary polynomial $g(x)$ of degree $n$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$. For a generic value $\lambda$, the equation $g(x) = \lambda$ has $n$ solutions. The question is: are these $n$ solutions *generically distinct*, or do some always collapse together?

The answer comes from the derivative. Two roots of $g(x) - \lambda$ coincide exactly when $g(x) - \lambda$ has a *repeated* root, which is exactly when $g(x) - \lambda$ and its derivative $g'(x)$ share a root.

Now, $g'(x)$ doesn't depend on $\lambda$. So if $g'(x)$ is identically zero, then $g(x) - \lambda$ has a repeated root for *every* $\lambda$ — every preimage is degenerate, the $n$ algebraic preimages always collapse to fewer distinct preimages.

If $g'(x)$ is not identically zero, then $g(x) - \lambda$ shares a root with $g'(x)$ only for the finitely many $\lambda$ where the relevant resultant vanishes, i.e., for finitely many "bad" values. For a generic $\lambda$, the $n$ preimages are distinct.

This is exactly the distinction:
- $R_1'(x) \not\equiv 0$: generic preimages are distinct. Kernel size = degree. **Separable.**
- $R_1'(x) \equiv 0$: preimages always collapse. Kernel size < degree. **Inseparable.**

### When can $R_1'(x) \equiv 0$ happen?

In characteristic 0 (working over $\mathbb{Q}$, $\mathbb{R}$, $\mathbb{C}$), the derivative of $x^n$ is $n x^{n-1}$, which vanishes only for $n = 0$. So a non-constant polynomial always has a non-zero derivative. Inseparability cannot happen.

In characteristic $p$, the derivative of $x^p$ is $p x^{p-1} = 0$. So $x^p$ is a non-constant polynomial with zero derivative. Same for $x^{2p}, x^{3p}, \ldots$ — any polynomial all of whose exponents are multiples of $p$ has zero derivative.

This is the only way for a polynomial's derivative to be identically zero (you can check term by term): every monomial $a_k x^k$ contributes $k a_k x^{k-1}$ to the derivative, and this vanishes only when $k a_k = 0$ in the field, i.e., either $a_k = 0$ or $p \mid k$.

So $R_1'(x) \equiv 0$ means every exponent in $R_1$ is a multiple of $p$. Equivalently, $R_1(x) = r_1(x^p)$ for some rational function $r_1$.

### The Frobenius factorization

When $R_1(x) = r_1(x^p)$, the map $\phi$ factors as
$$\phi(x, y) = (r_1(x^p), \ y \cdot R_2(x)).$$

The map $(x, y) \mapsto (x^p, y^p)$ — the **absolute Frobenius** — is itself a homomorphism in characteristic $p$ because $(u + v)^p = u^p + v^p$ (the binomial-coefficient argument, spelled out in § 0). So we can decompose: $\phi = \phi_{\text{sep}} \circ \pi$ where $\pi$ is the absolute Frobenius and $\phi_{\text{sep}}$ is the residual, which is separable because all its $p$-th-power structure has been peeled off.

This is the **decomposition theorem**: any isogeny factors uniquely as a separable isogeny composed with iterates of Frobenius. The "inseparable degree" of $\phi$ is just $p^r$ where $r$ counts how many times you peeled off a Frobenius.

> **Is there a separability analogue over $\mathbb{F}_p$ itself, not just for elliptic curves?**
> Yes — the concept comes from the theory of field extensions and polynomial factorization. A polynomial $g(x) \in \mathbb{F}_p[x]$ is called separable if $\gcd(g, g') = 1$, i.e., if $g$ has no repeated roots in any extension field. This is exactly the same derivative criterion as above. The polynomial $x^p - a$ is inseparable: it equals $(x - a^{1/p})^p$ in any field where $a^{1/p}$ exists, so it has just one root with multiplicity $p$. The isogeny version is the same idea, applied to the rational function describing the $x$-coordinate of the isogeny.

### The Frobenius map: which exponent belongs to which curve

The absolute Frobenius $\sigma_p(x) = x^p$ is the *building block* of inseparability — every inseparable isogeny factors through it. But when a curve $E$ is defined over $\mathbb{F}_q$ with $q = p^k$, the *curve Frobenius* $\pi_q: (x, y) \mapsto (x^q, y^q)$ used in Hasse's theorem, Schoof's algorithm, and the characteristic polynomial $\pi_q^2 - t \pi_q + q = 0$ is the full $q$-th-power map, not the $p$-th. It has degree $q$ (not $p$), and it factors as $k$ compositions of $\sigma_p$ with itself. This is why the constant term of the characteristic polynomial is $q$ rather than $p$: the Frobenius in question is the $q$-power map by definition, as explained in § 0.

## § 11. Torsion and the algebraic pictures

Why is an elliptic curve "two-dimensional"? Why does $E[n]$ have size $n^2$? Two algebraic viewpoints — rational functions and $2 \times 2$ matrices acting on torsion — describe the same object and both work over any field. A third viewpoint, complex lattices, gives geometric intuition but requires working over $\mathbb{C}$; it is deferred to § 15A.

### Picture 1: the rational-function viewpoint

Compute the multiplication-by-2 map on the $x$-coordinate. Take $P = (x, y)$; the slope of the tangent line at $P$ is $\lambda = (3x^2 + a)/(2y)$. The $x$-coordinate of $2P$ is
$$x_{2P} = \lambda^2 - 2x = \frac{(3x^2 + a)^2}{4y^2} - 2x = \frac{(3x^2 + a)^2 - 8x(x^3 + ax + b)}{4(x^3 + ax + b)}.$$

Numerator: $(3x^2 + a)^2 - 8x(x^3 + ax + b) = 9x^4 + 6ax^2 + a^2 - 8x^4 - 8ax^2 - 8bx = x^4 - 2ax^2 - 8bx + a^2$.

Denominator: $4(x^3 + ax + b)$, degree 3.

So the $x$-map of $[2]$ is a degree-4-over-degree-3 rational function. Taking the max: $\deg([2]) = 4 = 2^2$.

For $[3]$: applying the addition formula to $[2]P + P$, doing the algebra, you get a degree-9-over-degree-8 rational function. $\deg([3]) = 9 = 3^2$.

For $[n]$: by induction on the addition formulas, you find $\deg([n]) = n^2$.

The "$n^2$" is just what comes out when you crank the polynomial algebra. The $n^2$ has no deep meaning at this level — it's the answer to the explicit computation. But we want to *understand* why $n^2$, not just compute it.

> **A remark on the third picture.** Over the complex numbers there is a *third* viewpoint on torsion — the curve as a torus $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ with $[n]$ acting as multiplication of complex numbers. That viewpoint gives the deepest "why $n^2$" — but it needs machinery (Weierstrass $\wp$, double periodicity, the lattice $\Lambda$) that we haven't built yet. It is developed in § 15A. Everything in this section works over any field, including finite fields where no lattice exists directly.

### Picture 2: the $2 \times 2$ matrix viewpoint on torsion

We saw in Picture 1 that $\deg([n]) = n^2$. When $n$ is coprime to the characteristic $p$, the map $[n]$ is separable (its derivative $n$ is nonzero in the field), so by § 10, $|\ker([n])| = \deg([n]) = n^2$. The kernel of $[n]$ is by definition the $n$-torsion
$$E[n] = \{P \in E(\bar{K}) : [n]P = \mathcal{O}\},$$
so $|E[n]| = n^2$. That is the point-counting statement.

#### Why $E[\ell]$ has $\ell^2$ points, structurally

Beyond just counting, we can pin down the *group structure* of $E[\ell]$ for a prime $\ell \neq p$. Every $P \in E[\ell]$ satisfies $[\ell]P = \mathcal{O}$, so the order of $P$ divides $\ell$; since $\ell$ is prime, the order is either $1$ (only for $P = \mathcal{O}$) or exactly $\ell$.

$E[\ell]$ is a finite abelian group of order $\ell^2$ in which every non-identity element has order $\ell$. The classification of finite abelian groups gives only two options for a group of order $\ell^2$:
- $\mathbb{Z}/\ell^2\mathbb{Z}$ (cyclic), which contains elements of order $\ell^2$ — ruled out.
- $\mathbb{Z}/\ell\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/\ell\mathbb{Z}$ (two-dimensional over $\mathbb{F}_\ell$), in which every non-identity element has order $\ell$ — this is what we have.

Concretely: pick any non-identity $P_1 \in E[\ell]$. It generates a cyclic subgroup $H_1 = \{\mathcal{O}, P_1, [2]P_1, \ldots, [\ell-1]P_1\}$ of size $\ell$. Since $|E[\ell]| = \ell^2 > \ell$, there exists $P_2 \in E[\ell] \setminus H_1$. The pair $(P_1, P_2)$ is $\mathbb{F}_\ell$-linearly independent (if $[a]P_1 + [b]P_2 = \mathcal{O}$ with $b \neq 0$, then $P_2 \in H_1$, contradiction; so $b = 0$, and then $a P_1 = \mathcal{O}$ forces $a \equiv 0 \pmod \ell$). So every element of $E[\ell]$ is uniquely $[a]P_1 + [b]P_2$ with $a, b \in \mathbb{F}_\ell$:
$$E[\ell] \cong \mathbb{F}_\ell \oplus \mathbb{F}_\ell,$$
a two-dimensional $\mathbb{F}_\ell$-vector space with basis $\{P_1, P_2\}$.

The case $\ell = p$ is exceptional: since $[p]$ is inseparable (its derivative is $p = 0$ in the field), the kernel-size-equals-degree reasoning from § 10 fails. It turns out $E[p]$ is either trivial ($\{\mathcal{O}\}$) or a cyclic group of size $p$, depending on the curve; never $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^2$. The two-dimensional structure holds only for $\ell \neq p$, and that is the case we work with from here on.

#### Endomorphisms act as $2 \times 2$ matrices on $E[n]$

Any isogeny $\phi: E \to E$ sends $n$-torsion to $n$-torsion: if $[n]P = \mathcal{O}$, then $[n]\phi(P) = \phi([n]P) = \phi(\mathcal{O}) = \mathcal{O}$. So $\phi$ restricts to a group homomorphism $E[n] \to E[n]$. When $n = \ell$ is a prime $\neq p$, this is an $\mathbb{F}_\ell$-linear map on the two-dimensional space $E[\ell]$, and after picking a basis $\{P_1, P_2\}$ it is described by a $2 \times 2$ matrix
$$M_{\phi, \ell} = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \in M_2(\mathbb{F}_\ell), \qquad \phi(P_1) = [a]P_1 + [c]P_2, \quad \phi(P_2) = [b]P_1 + [d]P_2.$$
Same story for $E[n]$ with $n$ coprime to $p$: the matrix lives in $M_2(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})$.

#### Recovering $\phi$ from its matrix action, without machinery

A matrix on $E[n]$ over $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ is a snapshot — it doesn't uniquely determine $\phi$, because many isogenies of large degree can act the same way modulo a small $n$. To pin $\phi$ down, use progressively larger $n$. The natural sequence is $n = \ell, \ell^2, \ell^3, \ldots$ for a fixed prime $\ell$ (coprime to $p$). At each step, $E[\ell^r] \subset E[\ell^{r+1}]$ (any $\ell^r$-torsion point is in particular $\ell^{r+1}$-torsion), and multiplying an $\ell^{r+1}$-torsion point by $\ell$ gives an $\ell^r$-torsion point. The matrices $M_{\phi, \ell^r}$ on the successive $E[\ell^r]$ are consistent: the matrix at level $r+1$, viewed mod $\ell^r$, is exactly the matrix at level $r$.

The practical upshot: once $\ell^r$ exceeds the numerical quantities we care about (degree, trace), the matrix on $E[\ell^r]$ tells us the *exact integers* $\det(M_\phi)$ and $\mathrm{tr}(M_\phi)$, with no wraparound. So we can speak of "the determinant of $M_\phi$" and "the trace of $M_\phi$" as specific integers, not just residues.

#### Degree = determinant

We prove: for every prime $\ell \neq p$ and every $r \geq 1$,
$$\det(M_{\phi, \ell^r}) \equiv \deg(\phi) \pmod{\ell^r}.$$

Taking $r$ large enough (bigger than any $\ell$-power dividing $\deg\phi$) then gives $\det(M_\phi) = \deg(\phi)$ as integers, since both sides are non-negative and agree modulo an arbitrarily large power of $\ell$.

The proof compares two computations of the same set: the number of $\ell^r$-torsion points killed by $\phi$.

*From $\phi$.* Assume $\phi$ separable (which we can, since we've factored out inseparability via Frobenius in § 10, and the remaining inseparable part is coprime to $\ell$). Then $|\ker \phi| = \deg \phi$. The part of $\ker \phi$ that sits inside $E[\ell^r]$ is the $\ell^r$-torsion inside $\ker \phi$. Write $\deg \phi = \ell^s \cdot m$ with $\gcd(m, \ell) = 1$; then $\ker \phi$ has $\ell^s$ many $\ell$-power-torsion elements (the ones "sensitive" to the prime $\ell$), and for $r \geq s$ all of them fit inside $E[\ell^r]$. So
$$\bigl|\ker \phi \cap E[\ell^r]\bigr| = \ell^{\min(r,\ s)} \quad \text{where } s = v_\ell(\deg\phi).$$
Here $v_\ell(d)$ denotes the largest power of $\ell$ dividing the integer $d$.

*From the matrix.* Write $M_{\phi, \ell^r} = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}$ acting on $(\mathbb{Z}/\ell^r)^2$. Compute the kernel directly. Suppose first that at least one entry is a unit in $\mathbb{Z}/\ell^r$ (coprime to $\ell$); swap rows and columns so that entry sits in the top-left position — call it $a$. Since $a$ is invertible, row-reduce: subtract $(c/a) \cdot \text{Row 1}$ from Row 2, getting the upper-triangular form
$$\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ 0 & (ad - bc)/a \end{pmatrix} \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ 0 & \det(M)/a \end{pmatrix}.$$
The kernel is now $\{(x, y) : ax + by = 0 \text{ and } (\det M / a) \cdot y = 0\}$. The second equation constrains $y$ to be an element of $\mathbb{Z}/\ell^r$ annihilated by $\det M / a$; in a cyclic group of order $\ell^r$, the elements annihilated by $k$ form a subgroup of size $\gcd(k, \ell^r)$. Since $a$ is a unit, this is $\gcd(\det M, \ell^r) = \ell^{\min(r, v_\ell(\det M))}$. For each valid $y$, the first equation determines $x$ uniquely. So
$$|\ker M| = \ell^{\min(r, v_\ell(\det M))}.$$
If instead every entry of $M$ is divisible by $\ell$, factor out $\ell$: $M = \ell M'$, so $\det M = \ell^2 \det M'$ and the kernel of $M$ contains the kernel of $M'$ on $E[\ell^{r-1}]$; iterating this reduction gives the same formula.

*Equating.* Both counts must agree, so for $r$ large,
$$v_\ell(\det M_{\phi, \ell^r}) = v_\ell(\deg \phi).$$
This holds for every prime $\ell \neq p$. Since both quantities are non-negative integers and have the same $\ell$-adic valuation for every relevant $\ell$, they are equal as integers:
$$\det(M_\phi) = \deg(\phi).$$

#### Trace of the matrix defines the trace of the endomorphism

The matrix $M = M_{\phi, \ell^r}$ over $\mathbb{Z}/\ell^r$ satisfies its own characteristic polynomial (Cayley–Hamilton — elementary for $2 \times 2$: expand $M^2 - \mathrm{tr}(M) M + \det(M) I$ entry by entry and check it vanishes):
$$M^2 - \mathrm{tr}(M) M + \det(M) I \equiv 0 \pmod{\ell^r}.$$

Reading this back through the correspondence $M \leftrightarrow \phi|_{E[\ell^r]}$, and using $\det M \equiv \deg \phi \pmod{\ell^r}$:
$$\phi^2(P) - [\mathrm{tr}(M)] \phi(P) + [\deg \phi] P = \mathcal{O} \quad \text{for every } P \in E[\ell^r].$$

The integer $\mathrm{tr}(M)$ is consistent across different $r$ (matrices at level $r+1$ reduce mod $\ell^r$ to matrices at level $r$, so their traces agree mod $\ell^r$); taking $r$ larger than the trace itself, we get a specific integer $t := \mathrm{tr}(M_\phi)$ that doesn't wrap around.

**Independent of $\ell$.** The same integer $t$ comes out no matter which prime $\ell \neq p$ we use. Reason: if $t_\ell$ and $t_{\ell'}$ were the traces obtained via two different primes, then $\phi^2 - [t_\ell] \phi + [\deg \phi]$ vanishes on all $\ell$-power torsion, and $\phi^2 - [t_{\ell'}] \phi + [\deg \phi]$ vanishes on all $\ell'$-power torsion. Both together mean $(t_\ell - t_{\ell'}) \phi$ vanishes on infinitely many torsion points — and an endomorphism is determined by its action on any infinite set of torsion (its $x$-coordinate rational function has finitely many degrees of freedom, so equality on infinitely many points forces equality as a rational function). If $\phi$ is not the zero endomorphism, this forces $t_\ell = t_{\ell'}$ as integers.

So there is a single integer $t_\phi$ — the **trace of $\phi$** — with
$$\phi^2 - t_\phi \phi + \deg(\phi) \cdot [1] = 0 \quad \text{in } \mathrm{End}(E).$$

This is the definition of $t_\phi$; the connection to the dual isogeny ($t_\phi = \phi + \hat\phi$, § 12) and to point-counting for Frobenius ($t_\pi = q + 1 - N$, § 14) both follow from this identity, not the other way around.

#### Everything reduces to $2 \times 2$ linear algebra

Once the matrix viewpoint is in hand, the rest of the theory becomes elementary:
- **Degree** $= \det(M_\phi)$.
- **Trace** (in the sense above) $= \text{tr}(M_\phi)$.
- **Dual isogeny** $=$ adjugate matrix (§ 12).
- **Characteristic polynomial** $= \lambda^2 - \text{tr}(M) \lambda + \det(M)$.
- **Pairings** $=$ bilinear forms on the matrix (§ 13).

#### Why the matrix is a "high-compression" representation of $\phi$

For the Frobenius map, $\deg(\pi) = q$. If $q \approx 2^{256}$, the polynomial $x^q$ has an exponent of $2^{256}$ — but you can compute $x^q$ mod anything using $\lceil \log_2 q \rceil = 256$ square-and-multiply steps, so Frobenius is the rare "big-degree" polynomial you can evaluate cheaply.

For a **generic isogeny** $\phi$ used in post-quantum cryptography (§ 22), the degree can be around $2^{250}$, but $\phi$'s $x$-coordinate map is *not* a single monomial like $x^q$. It's a rational function whose numerator has degree $\sim 2^{250}$ with roughly that many distinct coefficients. Storing $\phi$ as a polynomial is impossible.

The matrix on $E[\ell^r]$ (for well-chosen $\ell^r$ of size $\sim 2^{256}$) is only four $\ell$-adic integers — a few tens of bytes. Given a basis of $E[\ell^r]$, the matrix determines how $\phi$ permutes torsion points, and that alone uniquely identifies the massive isogeny. This is what makes isogeny-based schemes representable: the *action on torsion* is compact even when the *rational function* is not.

## § 12. The dual isogeny

### Definition via the matrix picture

We have established: every endomorphism of $E$ acts on the $n$-torsion as a $2 \times 2$ matrix. For
$$M = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix},$$
the **adjugate** matrix (sometimes called the "classical adjoint") is
$$\hat{M} = \begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix}.$$

Direct multiplication:
$$M \hat{M} = \begin{pmatrix} ad - bc & -ab + ab \\ cd - cd & -bc + ad \end{pmatrix} = (ad - bc) I = \det(M) \cdot I.$$
$$M + \hat{M} = \begin{pmatrix} a + d & 0 \\ 0 & a + d \end{pmatrix} = \text{tr}(M) \cdot I.$$

The **dual isogeny** $\hat{\phi}$ of an isogeny $\phi$ is defined to be the endomorphism whose matrix is $\hat{M}_\phi$. Equivalently — and this is what makes the definition non-tautological — $\hat{\phi}$ is the unique isogeny such that
$$\hat{\phi} \circ \phi = [\deg \phi].$$

The two definitions agree because $\hat{M} M = \det(M) \cdot I$, and "multiplication by $\det(M)$" on the torsion is exactly the map $[\det M] = [\deg \phi]$.

### Where the dual comes from geometrically (separable case)

For a separable isogeny $\phi: E \to E'$ with kernel $K$ of size $d$, the curve $E$ is divided into $d$ cosets of $K$; $\phi$ sends each coset to a single point of $E'$. This makes $E'$ into the "quotient" $E/K$.

To define $\hat\phi: E' \to E$, we ask: given a point $Q$ on $E'$, what is the "best preimage" in $E$? There isn't a unique preimage — there are $d$ of them, forming a coset of $K$. The sum of those $d$ preimages, however, is a well-defined point in $E$ (since the group law makes sums of cosets meaningful). The map $Q \mapsto (\text{sum of all preimages})$ is the dual.

Composing: $\hat\phi(\phi(P)) = \sum_{P' \in P + K} P' = \sum_{k \in K}(P + k) = dP + \sum_{k \in K} k$. The sum $\sum_{k \in K} k$ vanishes in $K$ because for any abelian group, pairing $k$ with $-k$ collapses the sum to the sum of the elements satisfying $k = -k$ (the $2$-torsion of $K$), which for a "generic" kernel is $\mathcal{O}$; the degenerate cases (kernel containing exactly one nonzero $2$-torsion element) are handled by choosing the dual normalization appropriately. So $\hat\phi \circ \phi = [d]$, matching the matrix calculation.

### What the dual is good for, practically

Beyond the characteristic polynomial and pairings, the dual shows up in several concrete places:

1. **The only "near-inverse" we get for an isogeny.** Isogenies don't have inverses (an isogeny of degree > 1 is many-to-one). But $\hat\phi \circ \phi = [\deg \phi]$ — the dual composed with the original is just "multiply by the degree." In groups where the degree is invertible (i.e., coprime to the group order in the slot you care about), this lets you simulate an inverse map.

   **This is the engine of isogeny-based cryptography** (§ 22). The schemes SIDH, CSIDH, and SQIsign all work by walking through a graph whose vertices are elliptic curves and whose edges are isogenies. A scalar multiplication in this graph translates to a chain of isogenies $\phi_1, \phi_2, \ldots, \phi_n$. To traverse the chain backwards — for example, to "undo" a step you've taken, or to verify that a chain composes correctly — you use the dual at each step. Without duals, isogeny graphs would be one-way streets, and the schemes would be impossible to construct.

2. **It's the "transpose" of an isogeny with respect to the Weil pairing.** The relation $e_n(\phi(P), Q) = e_n(P, \hat\phi(Q))$ shows up wherever you need to factor through a pairing. **Identity-based encryption** (Boneh–Franklin) uses this: encryption involves a pairing $e(P_{\rm pub}, H(\text{id}))$, and security reductions move the secret-key term between slots by replacing it with its dual. **BLS signatures** (Boneh–Lynn–Shacham, used in Ethereum's consensus layer) verify by checking $e(\sigma, G) = e(H(m), \text{pk})$, and the proofs of unforgeability move terms between slots using the dual relationship. So the dual isn't just a curiosity for pairing-based crypto — it's the algebraic tool that makes the security arguments go through.

3. **It builds new isogenies from old.** Given $\phi: E \to E'$, the dual $\hat\phi: E' \to E$ is essentially the only structural map you can construct going back. Composing $\phi$ with $\hat\phi$ gives $[\deg \phi]$; composing $\hat\phi$ with another isogeny gives new isogenies. The whole theory of isogeny graphs (e.g., the **Volcano structure** of supersingular isogeny graphs, used in SIDH) is built on these compositions.

4. **It's what makes additivity of the degree pairing work.** We'll use $\widehat{(\phi + \psi)} = \hat\phi + \hat\psi$ in § 13 to derive the pairing formula. The proof is just $\widehat{M + N} = \hat M + \hat N$ at the matrix level — but this is what gives the pairing its bilinearity.

### Three identities

Putting everything in one place:
$$\phi + \hat\phi = [\text{tr}(\phi)] \quad (\text{trace} \in \mathbb{Z}),$$
$$\phi \circ \hat\phi = \hat\phi \circ \phi = [\deg(\phi)],$$
$$\widehat{\phi + \psi} = \hat\phi + \hat\psi.$$

All three come from the same matrix identities for the adjugate. The "trace" here is the trace of $\phi$ as a $2 \times 2$ matrix on the $n$-torsion (§ 11); this is the same trace as appears in $N = p + 1 - t$ for Frobenius, as we'll verify in § 14.

## § 13. Pairings: what they actually are

This section demystifies pairings: what the underlying concept is, where it shows up on elliptic curves, and why anyone uses them.

### What a pairing fundamentally is

A **pairing** is a bilinear function of two arguments:
$$\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle: A \times B \to C$$
satisfying
$$\langle a_1 + a_2, b \rangle = \langle a_1, b \rangle + \langle a_2, b \rangle,$$
$$\langle a, b_1 + b_2 \rangle = \langle a, b_1 \rangle + \langle a, b_2 \rangle,$$
and similar for scaling.

That's it. A pairing is "a way of multiplying two things together that distributes over addition in each slot." Examples you already know:
- **Dot product**: $\langle u, v \rangle = u \cdot v$. Bilinear pairing $\mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$.
- **Matrix multiplication**: $(A, B) \mapsto AB$. Bilinear pairing.
- **Determinant of a $2 \times 2$ matrix from its columns**: $(u, v) \mapsto \det[u | v]$. Bilinear pairing $\mathbb{R}^2 \times \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$. (This is alternating: $\det[v | u] = -\det[u | v]$.)
- **Integration**: $(f, g) \mapsto \int f g$. Bilinear pairing of function spaces.

The common theme: a pairing takes two objects and produces a single "scalar-like" output, in a way that respects the linear structure of both inputs.

### Pairings on elliptic curves: what they could possibly do for us

If we have a pairing $e: E[n] \times E[n] \to (\text{some abelian group})$, it gives us a way to "multiply" two torsion points together. What's that good for?

Suppose the output is in a finite cyclic group, like $\mathbb{F}_q^*$. Then for $P, Q \in E[n]$:
$$e(aP, bQ) = e(P, Q)^{ab}$$
(by bilinearity, $e(aP, bQ) = a \cdot b \cdot e(P, Q) = (e(P,Q))^{ab}$ in multiplicative notation for the target).

So: if I know $aP$ and $bQ$ but want to know $ab$, I can compute $e(aP, bQ)$ and try to take the discrete log in the target group. This is the **MOV attack** in its purest form: a pairing translates the discrete log on $E$ into a discrete log in $\mathbb{F}_q^*$. If $\mathbb{F}_q^*$'s discrete log is easier (which it often is, via sub-exponential index-calculus), the elliptic curve's discrete log becomes easier.

So pairings are a sword that cuts both ways: they're a powerful constructive tool (let you build new protocols like identity-based encryption) and a security threat (let you attack curves with small embedding degree).

### The degree pairing on endomorphisms

The pairing
$$\langle \phi, \psi \rangle = \deg(\phi - \psi) - \deg(\phi) - \deg(\psi)$$
is bilinear on $\text{End}(E)$, and it has direct cryptographic consequences (it gives the Hasse bound on the number of points, below).

**Bilinearity from matrix algebra.** We have $\deg(\phi) = \det(M_\phi)$. Now:
$$\det(M - N) = \det(M) + \det(N) - \text{tr}(\hat M N)$$
(this is a $2 \times 2$ matrix identity — easy to check by expansion). Rearranging:
$$\det(M - N) - \det(M) - \det(N) = -\text{tr}(\hat M N).$$

So $\langle \phi, \psi \rangle = -\text{tr}(\hat M_\phi M_\psi)$. The trace is linear in $M_\psi$ and the adjugate is linear in $M_\phi$, so this is bilinear.

Concretely: if $\phi$ has matrix $\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}$ and $\psi$ has matrix $\begin{pmatrix} a' & b' \\ c' & d' \end{pmatrix}$, then
$$\hat M_\phi M_\psi = \begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} a' & b' \\ c' & d' \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} da' - bc' & db' - bd' \\ -ca' + ac' & -cb' + ad' \end{pmatrix}.$$
Trace: $(da' - bc') + (ad' - cb') = ad' + a'd - bc' - b'c$. Linear in $a', b', c', d'$ and linear in $a, b, c, d$. Bilinear.

This is the **polarization identity** from quadratic forms: if $Q$ is a quadratic form, the bilinear form $B(x, y)$ such that $Q(x + y) = Q(x) + Q(y) + 2 B(x, y)$ is recovered by
$$B(x, y) = \frac{1}{2}(Q(x + y) - Q(x) - Q(y)).$$

In our setting, $\deg$ is the quadratic form (it's $\det$, a quadratic polynomial in the matrix entries), and $\langle \phi, \psi \rangle = \deg(\phi - \psi) - \deg(\phi) - \deg(\psi)$ extracts the bilinear shadow. (The minus sign instead of plus, and the factor of 2, are sign conventions.)

### From the degree pairing to the Hasse bound

With $\psi = \phi$:
$$\langle \phi, \phi \rangle = \deg(0) - 2 \deg(\phi) = -2 \deg(\phi)$$
(using $\deg(0) = 0$ for the zero map). Cross-checking via the matrix form: $\langle \phi, \phi \rangle = -\text{tr}(\hat M_\phi M_\phi) = -\text{tr}(\det(M_\phi) I) = -2 \det(M_\phi) = -2 \deg(\phi)$. Consistent.

So the pairing is negative-definite on the diagonal — but its negation $-\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ is positive-definite. Any positive-definite bilinear form $B$ satisfies a **Cauchy-Schwarz inequality** $|B(\phi, \psi)|^2 \leq B(\phi, \phi) \cdot B(\psi, \psi)$. The proof is a one-liner: for any scalar $\alpha$, $B(\alpha\phi + \psi, \alpha\phi + \psi) \geq 0$. Expanding: $\alpha^2 B(\phi, \phi) + 2\alpha B(\phi, \psi) + B(\psi, \psi) \geq 0$ as a quadratic in $\alpha$. A non-negative quadratic has non-positive discriminant, so $4 B(\phi, \psi)^2 - 4 B(\phi, \phi) B(\psi, \psi) \leq 0$, giving the inequality.

Applied to our negated pairing: $|\langle \phi, \psi \rangle|^2 \leq \langle \phi, \phi \rangle \langle \psi, \psi \rangle = 4 \deg(\phi) \deg(\psi)$.

Specializing to $\phi = \pi$ (Frobenius) and $\psi = [1]$ (identity):
- $\deg(\pi) = q$ (Frobenius has $x$-coordinate map $x \mapsto x^q$, degree $q$).
- $\deg([1]) = 1$.
- $\langle \pi, [1] \rangle = \deg(\pi - 1) - q - 1$.
- $\deg(\pi - 1) = |\ker(\pi - 1)|$ in the separable case (and $\pi - 1$ *is* separable: the derivative of $\pi - 1$ on $x$-coordinate is the derivative of $x^q - x$, which is $-1$, non-zero). The kernel is the fixed-point set of Frobenius, i.e., $E(\mathbb{F}_q)$, of size $N$.

So $\langle \pi, [1] \rangle = N - q - 1 = -t$ (using $N = q + 1 - t$).

Plugging into Cauchy-Schwarz:
$$t^2 \leq 4 q, \quad \text{i.e.,} \quad |t| \leq 2 \sqrt{q}.$$

This is the **Hasse bound**: the number of points on $E$ over $\mathbb{F}_q$ stays within $2\sqrt{q}$ of $q + 1$. It follows from Cauchy-Schwarz applied to the degree pairing — no deeper theory required.

### The Weil pairing

The Weil pairing is a specific pairing
$$e_n: E[n] \times E[n] \to \mu_n,$$
where $\mu_n$ is the group of $n$-th roots of unity in the base field. It has four properties:

- **Bilinear**: $e_n(P_1 + P_2, Q) = e_n(P_1, Q) e_n(P_2, Q)$ and similarly in the second slot.
- **Alternating**: $e_n(P, P) = 1$. (So pairing a torsion point with itself gives no information — the pairing only "sees" the way two *different* points sit relative to each other inside the 2D torsion space.)
- **Non-degenerate**: if $e_n(P, Q) = 1$ for all $Q \in E[n]$, then $P = \mathcal{O}$. (No "ghost" torsion points hidden from the pairing.)
- **Respects field automorphisms**: if $\sigma$ is any automorphism of the base field that fixes the curve's coefficients (e.g., the Frobenius $z \mapsto z^q$, which fixes $\mathbb{F}_q$), then $e_n(\sigma P, \sigma Q) = \sigma(e_n(P, Q))$. In practical terms: the pairing's output transforms the same way under field automorphisms as its inputs. This is what makes pairings well-defined as field-intrinsic operations and not artifacts of coordinate choices.

You can think of it as the "determinant pairing" on $E[n]$. Over $\mathbb{C}$ with the lattice picture (§ 15A): $E[n]$ is generated by $\omega_1/n$ and $\omega_2/n$. The pairing of these two generators is the primitive $n$-th root of unity $e^{2\pi i/n}$, and the pairing of $aP_1 + bP_2$ and $cP_1 + dP_2$ is $e^{2\pi i (ad - bc)/n}$ — literally a determinant exponentiated.

That's why it transforms like a determinant under isogenies: an isogeny $\phi$ acts on $E[n]$ by a matrix $M$, and
$$e_n(\phi P, \phi Q) = e_n(P, Q)^{\det M} = e_n(P, Q)^{\deg \phi}.$$

### The Tate pairing: what problem it solves

The Weil pairing's drawback: it pairs $E[n] \times E[n]$ and lands in $n$-th roots of unity. In characteristic $p$, computing it requires both inputs to be $n$-torsion points, but $E[n]$ generally lives over a strictly larger field than $\mathbb{F}_q$ itself. The smallest extension $\mathbb{F}_{q^k}$ that contains $E[n]$ has $k$ determined by: $k$ is the smallest positive integer such that $n$ divides $q^k - 1$. (Reason: $n$-th roots of unity live in $\mathbb{F}_{q^k}$ iff $n \mid |\mathbb{F}_{q^k}^*| = q^k - 1$, and full $E[n]$ requires both the roots of unity and the corresponding curve points.) This $k$ is the **embedding degree** of the curve with respect to $n$. For some curves $k$ is small (12 for BLS12-381, useful); for others it's exponentially large (most random curves, undesirable for pairing-friendly use but desirable for Weil-pairing-resistance).

The Tate pairing
$$\tau_n: E(\mathbb{F}_q)[n] \times E(\mathbb{F}_q) / n E(\mathbb{F}_q) \to \mathbb{F}_{q^k}^* / (\mathbb{F}_{q^k}^*)^n$$
solves this asymmetry. It only requires the *first* slot to be $n$-torsion (which can live in $\mathbb{F}_q$ directly), with the second slot being any point modulo $n$-torsion images. The output is a coset in the multiplicative group, which after raising to the $(q^k - 1)/n$ power becomes a clean $n$-th root of unity.

**What this buys you concretely**: the Tate pairing is computationally cheaper than the Weil pairing for curves of practical cryptographic interest, because you can avoid working in the full $n$-torsion extension field. You only need an $n$-torsion point in $\mathbb{F}_q$ (you have one, by design of the curve) and any other point.

**Where pairings come from**: both pairings are computed using **Miller's algorithm**, which evaluates a certain rational function $f_{n, P}$ on a divisor of points. The Miller function has roots and poles at carefully-chosen torsion points. You can think of $f_{n, P}$ as the "characteristic function" of the point $P$'s $n$-th-power structure — it encodes the information $P \in E[n]$ in the form of a rational function on $E$. Evaluating this function at $Q$ extracts the pairing.

This is intimidating sounding because of the divisors, but the core idea is just: rational functions on the curve have prescribed roots and poles, and the pattern of roots and poles around an $n$-torsion point can be encoded into a number by evaluating at another point. The "loop exponent" is the $n$ in $f_{n, P}$, the "Miller function" is $f_{n, P}$ itself, and "formal sums of points" are just bookkeeping for which roots and poles you have where.

> **The unifying point**: every pairing on elliptic curves is some kind of bilinear extraction from the group structure. The degree pairing extracts the *determinant of the difference* of two endomorphisms. The Weil pairing extracts the *determinant of the action on $n$-torsion*. The Tate pairing extracts a clever rational-function shadow. All three are bilinear functions of pairs of points (or endomorphisms), differing only in *what* they extract and *where* the output lives.

## § 14. The characteristic polynomial of Frobenius

We've assembled the pieces; now we put them together.

### The characteristic polynomial
For any endomorphism $\phi : E \to E$, there is a $2 \times 2$ matrix $M_\phi$ describing its action on any $\ell$-power torsion $E[\ell^r]$ with $\ell$ coprime to $p$ (§ 11). Any $2 \times 2$ matrix satisfies its own characteristic polynomial (Cayley–Hamilton):
$$M_\phi^2 - \text{tr}(M_\phi) M_\phi + \det(M_\phi) I = 0.$$
We've seen that $\deg(\phi) = \det(M_\phi)$ and that Tate-module matrices were constructed to reflect the endomorphism's action on the basis points. So the matrix identity translates back into an identity on endomorphisms themselves via the substitutions $M_\phi \to \phi$, $I \to [1]$ (the identity endomorphism, whose matrix is $I$), and $0 \to [0]$ (the zero endomorphism). The result:
$$\phi^2 - \operatorname{tr}(\phi) \phi + \deg(\phi) \cdot [1] = 0 \quad \text{in } \text{End}(E),$$
where $\operatorname{tr}(\phi)$ is defined as the integer $\operatorname{tr}(M_\phi)$ (which by § 11 doesn't depend on which $E[n]$ one uses, provided $n$ is coprime to the characteristic).

### The Frobenius endomorphism

For an elliptic curve $E$ defined over $\mathbb{F}_q$ (with $q = p^k$), the **Frobenius endomorphism** is
$$\pi: E \to E, \quad (x, y) \mapsto (x^q, y^q).$$

A few facts to establish:

1. **$\pi$ is a homomorphism**: this follows from $(u + v)^q = u^q + v^q$ in characteristic $p$ (iterate $(u+v)^p = u^p + v^p$ from § 0 a total of $k$ times). The chord-and-tangent law involves rational expressions in the coordinates; applying $\pi$ commutes with those expressions because raising to the $q$-th power preserves *both* addition and multiplication in the field. So $\pi(P + Q) = \pi(P) + \pi(Q)$.

2. **$\pi$ has degree $q$**: the $x$-coordinate map is $x \mapsto x^q$, a polynomial of degree $q$. So $\deg(\pi) = q$.

3. **$\pi$ is inseparable**: the formal derivative of $x^q$ is $q x^{q-1} = 0$ in characteristic $p$ (since $q = p^k$ and $p \equiv 0$). So $\pi$ is purely inseparable.

Plugging $\phi = \pi$, $\deg(\phi) = q$ into the general endomorphism identity from the previous subsection gives the **Frobenius characteristic polynomial**:
$$\pi^2 - t \pi + q \cdot [1] = 0 \quad \text{in } \text{End}(E),$$
where $t = \operatorname{tr}(M_\pi)$ is the integer trace of Frobenius's action on any $E[\ell]$ (for $\ell$ coprime to $p$). It remains to identify this $t$ with the $t$ from point-counting in § 5.

### Why $t$ here equals the $t$ in $N = q + 1 - t$

A point $(x, y)$ lies in $E(\mathbb{F}_q)$ if and only if its coordinates are fixed by every field automorphism of $\bar{\mathbb{F}}_q$ that fixes $\mathbb{F}_q$ — equivalently, fixed by $\pi$: $\pi(x, y) = (x, y).$

**These are fixed points.** Counting $N_q = |E(\mathbb{F}_q)|$ is exactly counting the fixed points of $\pi$ on the curve.

**Deriving $N_q = \det(I - M_\pi)$ directly.** A point is fixed by $\pi$ iff $(\pi - [1])(P) = \mathcal{O}$, so
$$N_q = |E(\mathbb{F}_q)| = |\ker(\pi - [1])|.$$
The map $\pi - [1]$ is separable: its formal derivative on the $x$-coordinate is the derivative of $x^q - x$, which is $q x^{q-1} - 1 = -1$ (nonzero in the field, since $q \equiv 0$ but $-1 \not\equiv 0 \pmod p$). By § 10 (kernel size = degree for separable maps),
$$|\ker(\pi - [1])| = \deg(\pi - [1]).$$

By § 11 Picture 2, degree = determinant of the matrix, so
$$\deg(\pi - [1]) = \det(M_\pi - I).$$

For $2 \times 2$ matrices, $\det(M - I) = \det(I - M)$ (because $\det(-A) = (-1)^2 \det(A) = \det(A)$ in dimension $2$). So
$$N_q = \det(I - M_\pi).$$

**Computing the determinant via the characteristic polynomial.** The characteristic polynomial of $M_\pi$ is $\det(\lambda I - M_\pi) = \lambda^2 - \text{tr}(M_\pi) \lambda + \det(M_\pi)$. Evaluating at $\lambda = 1$:
$$\det(I - M_\pi) = 1 - \text{tr}(M_\pi) + \det(M_\pi).$$

Substituting $\det(M_\pi) = \deg(\pi) = q$:
$$N_q = 1 - \text{tr}(M_\pi) + q.$$

Isolating the trace:
$$\text{tr}(M_\pi) = q + 1 - N_q.$$

Comparing with $t = q + 1 - N_q$ from § 5: the matrix trace of Frobenius on the torsion equals the trace-from-point-counting. The name "trace of Frobenius" for $t$ is finally justified.

> [!info] Alternative viewpoint via the degree pairing
>
> A second, equivalent route: the degree pairing $\langle \phi, \psi \rangle = \deg(\phi - \psi) - \deg(\phi) - \deg(\psi)$ from § 13, applied to $\pi$ and $[1]$:
$$\langle \pi, [1] \rangle = \deg(\pi - [1]) - q - 1 = N - q - 1 = -t.$$
On the other hand, on the matrix side, the degree pairing computes as $-\text{tr}(\hat M_\pi \cdot I) = -\text{tr}(M_\pi)$ (using $\hat M \cdot I = \hat M$ and the trace-of-adjugate identity $\text{tr}(\hat M) = \text{tr}(M)$ for $2 \times 2$). So $\langle \pi, [1] \rangle = -\text{tr}(M_\pi)$, giving $\text{tr}(M_\pi) = t$ once more. Both derivations converge on the same integer.

### Frobenius Trace as an Error Term

We treat $t = q + 1 - N_q$ as an error term, since we expect the elliptic curve to have roughly as many $x$-coordinates as elements in our base field, giving $q + 1$ points total (where the $+1$ accounts for the point at infinity). Hence, $t$ measures the deviation between this expected value $q + 1$ and the true number of points $N_q$.
Moreover, by the Hasse bound: $|t| \leq 2\sqrt{q}$; the error term $t$ is tightly constrained within a "random walk" window of $\pm 2\sqrt{q}$.


### The trace formula in context

We have several equivalent characterizations of the trace $t$ of Frobenius:

| Description                            | Formula                                                                                         |
| -------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Point counting**                     | $t = q + 1 - \#E(\mathbb{F}_q)$                                                                 |
| **Sum of Legendre symbols**            | $t = -\sum_{x \in \mathbb{F}_p} \left( \frac{f(x)}{p} \right)$ (for $q = p$)                    |
| **Hasse invariant**                    | $t \equiv c_{p-1} \pmod p$, where $c_{p-1}$ is the coefficient of $x^{p-1}$ in $f(x)^{(p-1)/2}$ |
| **Matrix trace**                       | $t = \text{tr}(M_\pi)$, the trace of Frobenius acting on any $E[\ell^r]$ with $\ell \neq p$    |
| **Characteristic polynomial constant** | $\pi^2 - t \pi + q = 0$                                                                         |
| **Discriminant-style bound**           | $t \leq 2 \sqrt{q}$ (Hasse)                                                                     |

All six describe the same integer. They serve different purposes:
- Counting and Legendre sum are how you'd compute $t$ for small $p$.
- Hasse invariant gives you $t \mod p$ from the coefficients, with no enumeration.
- Matrix and characteristic polynomial are how you reason about $t$ structurally.
- Schoof's algorithm computes $t \mod \ell$ for many small $\ell$, then CRT.


### Why $t \equiv 0 \pmod p$ is the definition of supersingular

We promised in § 6 that the condition "$t \equiv 0 \pmod p$" — the one detected by the Hasse invariant — is more than a numerical curiosity. Here is what it actually means: it is equivalent to $E[p] = \{\mathcal{O}\}$, an exceptional collapse of the $p$-torsion.

**The $p$-torsion has two possible shapes.** For a prime $\ell \neq p$, § 11 gave $E[\ell] \cong (\mathbb{Z}/\ell)^2$. For $\ell = p$ the same argument fails because $[p]$ is inseparable. Compute $|E[p]|$ directly via the composition $\pi \hat\pi = [\deg \pi] = [p]$ from § 12:
$$|E[p]| = |\ker[p]| = |\ker(\pi \circ \hat\pi)| = |\ker \hat\pi|,$$
using that $\pi$ has trivial kernel (purely inseparable, degree $p$). Now $\hat\pi$ has degree $p$, so its inseparable degree is either $1$ or $p$, and correspondingly $|\ker \hat\pi|$ is either $p$ or $1$:
$$E[p] \cong \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z} \quad (\hat\pi \text{ separable, "ordinary" curve}), \qquad E[p] = \{\mathcal{O}\} \quad (\hat\pi \text{ purely inseparable, "supersingular" curve}).$$

**Connecting to $t \bmod p$.** Every isogeny $\phi$ induces a scalar $d\phi$ in the ground field, capturing how $\phi$ scales the infinitesimal direction at $\mathcal{O}$: for $[n]$ the scalar is $n$, and for purely inseparable $\phi$ it is $0$ (this is exactly what § 10 meant by "the $x$-coordinate's derivative vanishes"). The scalar is additive under the group law: $d(\phi + \psi) = d\phi + d\psi$ (because the addition on $E$ is a smooth map, so its differential at the identity is a linear operation).

Apply this to $[t] = \pi + \hat\pi$:
$$t \equiv d\pi + d\hat\pi \equiv 0 + d\hat\pi \pmod p,$$
since $d\pi = 0$ (Frobenius is purely inseparable). So
$$t \equiv 0 \pmod p \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad d\hat\pi = 0 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \hat\pi \text{ purely inseparable} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad E[p] = \{\mathcal{O}\}.$$
The "$t \equiv 0 \pmod p$" of § 6, the "$\hat\pi$ purely inseparable" here, and the "no nontrivial $p$-torsion" of the top of this subsection are three names for the same phenomenon.

**Downstream consequences.** Supersingular curves are rare — roughly $p/12$ isomorphism classes over $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}$ — and have far richer endomorphism rings than ordinary curves: instead of the rank-$2$ commutative ring $\text{End}(E) = \mathbb{Z}[\alpha]$ (an order in an imaginary quadratic field), it becomes a rank-$4$ *non*-commutative ring (an order in a quaternion algebra). This richness makes supersingular curves the natural setting for **isogeny-based post-quantum cryptography** (§ 22): the supersingular isogeny graph is highly connected and easy to walk in one direction, hard to reverse. Historically, supersingular curves also appeared in early pairing-based cryptography, until the MOV attack (§ 18) exploited their small embedding degree.


### Frobenius eigenvalues
The eigenvalues of $M_\pi$ are the roots of the characteristic polynomial $\lambda^2 - t\lambda + q = 0$:

$$\lambda = \frac{t \pm \sqrt{t^2 - 4q}}{2}.$$

The Hasse bound $|t| \leq 2\sqrt{q}$ (derived in § 13 from Cauchy-Schwarz on the degree pairing) gives $t^2 \leq 4q$, so $t^2 - 4q \leq 0$. The discriminant is non-positive, so the square root is imaginary (or zero), and the roots are complex conjugates:
$$\lambda = \frac{t \pm i\sqrt{4q - t^2}}{2}.$$
Compute the magnitude:
$$|\lambda|^2 = \lambda \cdot \bar{\lambda} = \frac{t^2 + (4q - t^2)}{4} = \frac{4q}{4} = q.$$
So $|\lambda| = \sqrt{q}$.
In polar coordinates, any complex number can be written as its length multiplied by an angle direction ($e^{i\theta}$). Therefore, the two roots are:
$$\alpha = \sqrt{q} e^{i\theta}, \quad \beta = \sqrt{q} e^{-i\theta}$$
They sit directly opposite each other on the boundary of a perfect circle of radius $\sqrt{q}$.
In linear algebra, any $2 \times 2$ matrix that has a determinant of $1$ and complex-conjugate eigenvalues of length $1$ is structurally locked to a very specific shape. It must be equivalent to a standard trigonometry rotation matrix:

$$M_{\text{normalized}} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix}$$

This means that if we ignore the uniform expansion factor $\sqrt{q}$, the Frobenius map does not warp, skew, or flatten the grid of points. It simply picks the grid up and rotates it by a fixed angle $\theta$. It is a Unitary matrix.

## § 15. Schoof's algorithm

The problem: compute the trace $t$ of Frobenius for a large elliptic curve. By Hasse, $|t| \leq 2\sqrt{q}$; for $q \approx 2^{256}$, $t$ is roughly a 128-bit number. Direct enumeration over $E(\mathbb{F}_q)$ is infeasible ($2^{256}$ operations).

Schoof's insight: compute $t \mod \ell$ for many small primes $\ell$, then reconstruct $t$ via the Chinese Remainder Theorem.

### The setup

The Frobenius satisfies $\pi^2 - t \pi + q = 0$ as a relation in $\text{End}(E)$. Restrict this to the $\ell$-torsion $E[\ell]$:
$$\pi^2(P) - t \pi(P) + q P = \mathcal{O}, \quad \text{for all } P \in E[\ell].$$

On $E[\ell]$, the integers $t$ and $q$ act through their reductions mod $\ell$: write $t_\ell = t \mod \ell$ and $q_\ell = q \mod \ell$. The equation becomes
$$\pi^2(P) + q_\ell P = t_\ell \pi(P), \quad \text{for all } P \in E[\ell].$$

We know everything in this equation *except* $t_\ell$. We want to solve for $t_\ell$.

### Why we can't just enumerate points of $E[\ell]$

The $\ell$-torsion points have coordinates in $\mathbb{F}_q$ only if $E[\ell] \subseteq E(\mathbb{F}_q)$, which is generally not the case. They live in some extension field $\mathbb{F}_{q^k}$ with $k$ potentially as large as $\ell^2 - 1$. For $\ell = 31$ and $q$ a 256-bit prime, $k$ could be around 900, meaning the extension field has elements of size $2^{256 \times 900}$ — astronomically large.

### The trick: symbolic computation modulo the division polynomial

A point on $E[\ell]$ has its $x$-coordinate satisfying a specific polynomial $\psi_\ell(x) \in \mathbb{F}_q[x]$ — the **$\ell$-th division polynomial**. This polynomial has degree $(\ell^2 - 1)/2$ for odd $\ell$ (it vanishes on the $x$-coordinates of all non-identity $\ell$-torsion points, of which there are $\ell^2 - 1$ paired up by $\pm$).

Schoof's idea: don't compute with actual $\ell$-torsion points. Compute with a "generic" point $P = (x, y)$ where $x$ is a formal variable, $y$ is determined by $y^2 = f(x)$, and **all polynomial computations are reduced modulo $\psi_\ell(x)$**. The resulting algebra is identical to algebra on $\ell$-torsion points, because:
- For an actual $\ell$-torsion point $P_0 = (x_0, y_0)$, $\psi_\ell(x_0) = 0$.
- So any polynomial expression evaluated at $P_0$ is congruent mod $\psi_\ell$ to its reduction.
- And reduction mod $\psi_\ell$ keeps polynomials at degree $< (\ell^2 - 1)/2$, which is manageable.

For $\ell = 31$: polynomial degree about 480. Manageable.

### The algorithm

For each small prime $\ell$:
1. Compute the LHS: $\pi^2(P) + q_\ell P$ as a pair of rational functions in $x$ (and $y$), reduced mod $\psi_\ell(x)$.
2. For each candidate $t_\ell \in \{0, 1, \ldots, \ell - 1\}$, compute the RHS: $t_\ell \pi(P)$, reduced mod $\psi_\ell(x)$.
3. Find the $t_\ell$ where LHS = RHS. That's the trace mod $\ell$.

Repeat for $\ell = 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, \ldots$ until $\prod \ell > 4\sqrt{q}$. Then CRT gives $t$.

### Why each piece is necessary

- **$\pi$ is just "raise to $q$":** Easy to compute. $\pi(P) = (x^q \mod \psi_\ell, y^q \mod \psi_\ell)$.
- **$q_\ell$:** Just reduce $q \mod \ell$, an easy integer arithmetic.
- **$t_\ell$:** Unknown, but we have only $\ell$ values to try — instant.

The total cost is polynomial in $\log q$ (rather than $q$ itself), which is the breakthrough that made Schoof's algorithm practical.

## § 15A. The complex-analytic viewpoint: elliptic curves as tori

The rational-function and Tate-module pictures of § 11 both work over any field. Over the complex numbers, a third picture becomes available that is far more geometric: the elliptic curve *is* a torus $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$, and every algebraic structure on the curve (points, endomorphisms, isogenies, torsion, the $j$-invariant) has a linear-algebra shadow on the lattice $\Lambda$. This section develops that picture from scratch — motivating the elliptic integral, deriving the double periodicity, connecting the algebraic $\phi$ to complex multiplication by an $\alpha$, and using the picture to understand the $j$-invariant as a coordinate on the space of grid shapes.

Nothing in this section is used to prove statements over finite fields (the Tate-module picture handled those in § 11). Its role is to give geometric intuition and to build the language of *complex multiplication* that appears in secp256k1 and drives the GLV method (§ 15B).

### Motivating the elliptic integral

For the unit circle $x^2 + y^2 = 1$, there is a natural coordinate: arc length. If you walk along the circle starting from $(1, 0)$ and travel a distance $u$, you land at $(\cos u, \sin u)$. The map $u \mapsto (\cos u, \sin u)$ turns the additive group $(\mathbb{R}, +)$ into the group of rotations of the circle: addition of angles is composition of rotations. In differential terms, arc length is
$$u = \int_0^x \frac{dx'}{\sqrt{1 - (x')^2}},$$
where the integrand $1/\sqrt{1 - x^2} = 1/y$ is the reciprocal of the $y$-coordinate. That reciprocal is not an accident.

**Why $1/y$ is the natural integrand.** On any curve $y = f(x)$, the tangent-vector at the point $(x, y)$ points in the direction $(1, y'(x))$, so the arc-length differential is $ds = \sqrt{1 + (y'(x))^2}\, dx$. That is not $1/y \, dx$. What *is* $1/y \, dx$ for is a different invariant: it is the differential form that becomes invariant under the group law. Concretely, on $y^2 = f(x)$, differentiate: $2y \, dy = f'(x)\, dx$, so
$$\frac{dx}{y} = \frac{2\, dy}{f'(x)}.$$
Both expressions equal the same 1-form. This form has the property that if you translate the curve by the group law $P \mapsto P + Q$, the pullback of $dx/y$ under the translation equals $dx/y$ again — it is translation-invariant on the group.

For the circle, "the group law is angle addition", and the invariant 1-form $dx/y$ integrated along the circle *is* the angle, i.e., arc length; the two notions happen to coincide. For an elliptic curve, they don't: the invariant form $dx/y$ integrates to a coordinate that linearizes the group law but is not arc length in the ordinary Euclidean sense. The general story: on any algebraic group, there is a natural (up to scalar) invariant 1-form, and integrating it gives a linearizing coordinate. For elliptic curves this coordinate is the complex variable $u$ we're about to define.

So: we integrate $dx/y$ not because of arc-length reasoning, but because $dx/y$ is *the* translation-invariant differential form on the elliptic curve, and integrating it produces the coordinate in which the group law becomes ordinary complex addition.

### Why the integral is doubly periodic

For the circle, $\int_0^{2\pi} dx/y$ traces out a full loop and returns to the start — a single period $2\pi$. The circle has *one* topological loop, and the invariant differential detects it as one period.

An elliptic curve over $\mathbb{C}$ has *two* independent loops, and the invariant differential detects them as two independent periods $\omega_1, \omega_2$. This is what "doubly periodic" means, and it is forced by topology, not chosen.

**The topological argument.** The curve $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$ over $\mathbb{C}$ is a two-sheeted branched cover of the $x$-plane $\mathbb{C}_x = \mathbb{C} \cup \{\infty\}$ via the projection $(x, y) \mapsto x$: for each $x$ there are two $y$-values $\pm\sqrt{f(x)}$, except at the branch points where $f(x) = 0$ (three finite roots, call them $e_1, e_2, e_3$) and at $x = \infty$ (a fourth branch point, since $\sqrt{f(x)} \sim x^{3/2}$ has a square-root singularity there). So the curve is a two-sheeted cover of the sphere $\mathbb{C}_x$ with four branch points.

Topologically, a two-sheeted cover with $2n$ branch points is a surface of genus $n - 1$ (Riemann-Hurwitz). Four branch points give genus $1$: a **torus**. A torus has *two* independent nontrivial loops — the two "cycles" going around the two directions — so any closed 1-form has two independent integrals along these cycles.

Call those two loop integrals $\omega_1$ and $\omega_2$:
$$\omega_1 = \oint_{\gamma_1} \frac{dx}{y}, \qquad \omega_2 = \oint_{\gamma_2} \frac{dx}{y}.$$
Deforming the path of integration by a loop $\gamma_1$ changes $u$ by $\omega_1$; by $\gamma_2$, by $\omega_2$. So the "value" of $u = \int (dx/y)$ is not a single complex number but a coset in $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$, where
$$\Lambda = \mathbb{Z}\omega_1 + \mathbb{Z}\omega_2$$
is the lattice generated by the two periods. The two periods are $\mathbb{R}$-linearly independent in $\mathbb{C}$ (equivalently, their ratio $\tau = \omega_2/\omega_1$ has nonzero imaginary part), because the two topological cycles on the torus are independent — otherwise the branched cover would have wrong genus.

**Contrast with the circle.** The circle is topologically a *circle*, genus $0$ from the viewpoint above (it's a two-sheeted cover of a segment with two branch points, not four). It has one nontrivial loop, one period, and the integrand $dx/\sqrt{1 - x^2}$ is singly periodic in the resulting coordinate. The elliptic curve has one *more* branch point (four total), which upgrades the genus from $0$ to $1$ and doubles the number of independent periods.

### The Weierstrass parameterization

Take the map
$$\Phi: \mathbb{C}/\Lambda \longrightarrow E(\mathbb{C}), \qquad u \longmapsto (\wp(u), \tfrac{1}{2}\wp'(u))$$
where $\wp$ is the **Weierstrass $\wp$-function** associated to $\Lambda$. The definition
$$\wp(u) = \frac{1}{u^2} + \sum_{\omega \in \Lambda \setminus \{0\}} \left[\frac{1}{(u - \omega)^2} - \frac{1}{\omega^2}\right]$$
is not something we need to work with directly. Two properties matter:

1. $\wp$ is meromorphic on $\mathbb{C}$, has a double pole at every lattice point (and nowhere else), and satisfies $\wp(u + \omega) = \wp(u)$ for all $\omega \in \Lambda$. So it descends to a well-defined function on the quotient $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$.
2. $\wp$ and its derivative $\wp'$ satisfy an algebraic relation
$$(\wp')^2 = 4\wp^3 - g_2 \wp - g_3,$$
where $g_2, g_3$ are specific complex numbers computed from $\Lambda$. This is exactly the equation of an elliptic curve (after the substitution $x = \wp$, $y = \wp'/2$, which puts the curve in short Weierstrass form with $a = -g_2/4$, $b = -g_3/4$).

So $\Phi$ sends the torus $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ bijectively onto the complex points of $E$: $\wp$ is the $x$-coordinate, $\wp'/2$ is the $y$-coordinate, and the group law on $E$ corresponds to ordinary complex addition on $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$.

**Every complex elliptic curve arises this way.** For any lattice $\Lambda \subset \mathbb{C}$ the construction gives a curve; conversely, for any complex elliptic curve $E$ there is a lattice $\Lambda$ (unique up to $\mathbb{C}^*$-scaling) with $E \cong \mathbb{C}/\Lambda$. The correspondence is a dictionary:

| Elliptic curve $E$ over $\mathbb{C}$ | Torus $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ |
|---|---|
| A point $P \in E$ | An element $u \in \mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ |
| The identity $\mathcal{O}$ | The origin $0 \in \mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ |
| $P + Q$ (chord-and-tangent) | $u_P + u_Q$ (ordinary $+$ in $\mathbb{C}$) |
| $-P = (x, -y)$ | $-u \in \mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ |
| $[n]P$ | $n u$ |
| $E[n]$ | $\frac{1}{n}\Lambda / \Lambda \cong (\mathbb{Z}/n)^2$ |
| An isogeny $\phi: E \to E'$ | Multiplication $u \mapsto \alpha u$ with $\alpha \Lambda \subseteq \Lambda'$ |

The last row is what the rest of this section unpacks: on the torus side, endomorphisms are just complex numbers.

### Endomorphisms as complex multiplication: $\alpha \Lambda \subseteq \Lambda$

An endomorphism of the torus $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ that fixes $0$ is a *holomorphic* map $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda \to \mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ preserving $0$. Any such map lifts to a holomorphic map $\mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{C}$ preserving $0$ — but a holomorphic map $\mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{C}$ that respects the additive structure $\Lambda$ (i.e., sends the lattice into itself) must be $\mathbb{C}$-linear: $u \mapsto \alpha u$ for some fixed $\alpha \in \mathbb{C}$. (A general holomorphic map wouldn't fix $\Lambda$; the linearity is forced by "the map must send lattice points to lattice points on the nose, and the origin to the origin.") The extra condition ensuring that $u \mapsto \alpha u$ descends to a map $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda \to \mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ is exactly
$$\alpha \Lambda \subseteq \Lambda.$$

**Connecting $\alpha$ to the algebraic endomorphism $\phi$.** Given $\phi: E \to E$ (a rational map from § 8), compose with the Weierstrass parameterization:
$$\begin{array}{ccc}
\mathbb{C}/\Lambda & \xrightarrow{\Phi} & E(\mathbb{C}) \\
\downarrow \alpha \cdot & & \downarrow \phi \\
\mathbb{C}/\Lambda & \xrightarrow{\Phi} & E(\mathbb{C})
\end{array}$$
The left column is "multiplication by $\alpha$ on the torus"; the right column is "$\phi$ on the curve." The diagram commutes: this is the definition of $\alpha$ from $\phi$ (chase $u \in \mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ around the square; the value of $\alpha$ is forced by $\Phi(\alpha u) = \phi(\Phi(u))$ for all $u$). Every algebraic endomorphism $\phi$ has exactly one such $\alpha$ attached to it, and the correspondence is a ring isomorphism:
$$\phi + \psi \leftrightarrow \alpha_\phi + \alpha_\psi, \qquad \phi \circ \psi \leftrightarrow \alpha_\phi \cdot \alpha_\psi.$$
So the endomorphism ring $\text{End}(E)$, an abstract algebraic object, becomes a *subring of $\mathbb{C}$* — a very concrete linear-algebra object.

### The integer matrix: why $\alpha \Lambda \subseteq \Lambda$ forces integer entries

The condition $\alpha \Lambda \subseteq \Lambda$ says: $\alpha \omega_1$ and $\alpha \omega_2$ are elements of $\Lambda$. But $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z}\omega_1 + \mathbb{Z}\omega_2$ by definition — every element of $\Lambda$ is an integer combination of the two basis vectors. So there exist integers $m, n, p, q$ with
$$\alpha \omega_1 = m \omega_1 + n \omega_2, \qquad \alpha \omega_2 = p \omega_1 + q \omega_2,$$
and this can be written as a matrix equation:
$$\alpha \begin{pmatrix} \omega_1 \\ \omega_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} m & n \\ p & q \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \omega_1 \\ \omega_2 \end{pmatrix}.$$

The entries $m, n, p, q$ are *forced* to be integers by the definition of $\Lambda$ — no continuity or symmetry argument is needed; it's the definition of the lattice. Any element of $\Lambda$ has a unique expansion as an integer combination of $\omega_1, \omega_2$ (that's what a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis means), so the coefficients read off are integers by construction. This gives the ring embedding
$$\text{End}(E) \hookrightarrow M_2(\mathbb{Z}), \qquad \phi \mapsto \begin{pmatrix} m & n \\ p & q \end{pmatrix}.$$

The image is not all of $M_2(\mathbb{Z})$: the matrix must correspond to a valid $\alpha \in \mathbb{C}$, which imposes one extra constraint (the two rows must be consistent with the *same* $\alpha$; see the CM discussion below).

**Restricting to $n$-torsion.** The $n$-torsion is $E[n] = \frac{1}{n}\Lambda / \Lambda$, parameterized by pairs $(j/n, k/n)$ with $j, k \in \{0, 1, \ldots, n-1\}$ — a rank-$2$ module over $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$. Multiplication by $\alpha$ acts on $(j/n)\omega_1 + (k/n)\omega_2$ by the *same* integer matrix $\begin{pmatrix} m & n \\ p & q \end{pmatrix}$, reduced mod $n$. So the Tate-module picture (§ 11 Picture 2) agrees with the lattice picture: the matrix on $E[n]$ is the reduction mod $n$ of the integer matrix on $\Lambda$.

**Degree = determinant, revisited.** On $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$, the map $u \mapsto \alpha u$ has kernel $\{u : \alpha u \in \Lambda\}/\Lambda = \alpha^{-1}\Lambda / \Lambda$. This kernel has size equal to the index $[\Lambda : \alpha \Lambda]$, which for a rank-$2$ lattice map with integer matrix $M$ is $|\det M|$ (from ordinary linear algebra: the change of volume of a lattice fundamental parallelogram under a linear map is the absolute value of the determinant). By § 10, for a separable isogeny the kernel size equals the degree. So $\deg \phi = |\det M_\phi|$ — the identity we established over finite fields via matrix action on torsion now falls out of ordinary Euclidean geometry on the torus.

### The $j$-invariant as a coordinate on grid shapes

Two lattices $\Lambda$ and $\Lambda'$ give isomorphic elliptic curves if and only if they are homothetic: $\Lambda' = c \Lambda$ for some $c \in \mathbb{C}^*$. Scaling by $c$ doesn't change the *shape* of the grid; only the shape matters. So the space of elliptic curves over $\mathbb{C}$ is the space of lattices modulo scaling.

Given any lattice $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z}\omega_1 + \mathbb{Z}\omega_2$, rescale by $c = 1/\omega_1$: the new lattice is $\mathbb{Z} + \mathbb{Z}\tau$ where $\tau = \omega_2/\omega_1$. So every lattice is (up to homothety) determined by a single complex number $\tau$ with $\text{Im}(\tau) \neq 0$ — the ratio of periods. Conventionally we take $\text{Im}(\tau) > 0$ (upper half-plane).

The **$j$-invariant** is a function $j: \{\tau : \text{Im}(\tau) > 0\} \to \mathbb{C}$ that assigns to each lattice-shape a complex number, in such a way that
$$j(\tau) = j(\tau') \iff \text{the lattices } \mathbb{Z} + \mathbb{Z}\tau \text{ and } \mathbb{Z} + \mathbb{Z}\tau' \text{ describe the same elliptic curve.}$$

Concretely, the analytic formula is $j(\tau) = 1728 \cdot g_2^3 / (g_2^3 - 27 g_3^2)$ where $g_2, g_3$ are the coefficients of the Weierstrass equation associated to $\tau$; on plugging in and clearing, this is the same $j$ as the algebraic one:
$$j = 1728 \cdot \frac{4a^3}{4a^3 + 27b^2}.$$

So the $j$-invariant is a coordinate on the space of grid shapes: two grids give the same $j$ iff they describe the same curve.

**Why does the algebraic $j$ formula separate curves?** The invariance under $(a, b) \to (a/u^4, b/u^6)$ (§ 8's derivation) is the *same* invariance as $\tau \to \tau$ under rescaling of $\Lambda$: both are saying "isomorphic curves have the same shape." The concrete $j$ formula was chosen so that it's a *bijection* between isomorphism classes and complex numbers.

### The change-of-basis: $\tau \to \frac{a\tau + b}{c\tau + d}$

A given lattice $\Lambda$ has many equivalent $\mathbb{Z}$-bases: any $(\omega_1', \omega_2')$ with $\omega_1' = a\omega_1 + b\omega_2$, $\omega_2' = c\omega_1 + d\omega_2$ for integers $a, b, c, d$ satisfying $ad - bc = \pm 1$ is also a basis of $\Lambda$ (this determinant condition ensures the change is invertible over $\mathbb{Z}$). Different basis choices give different $\tau = \omega_2/\omega_1$:
$$\tau' = \frac{\omega_2'}{\omega_1'} = \frac{c\omega_1 + d\omega_2}{a\omega_1 + b\omega_2} = \frac{c + d\tau}{a + b\tau}.$$
Convention flips the roles of the letters to match the standard: writing the change of basis as $\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \in \text{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$, one gets
$$\tau' = \frac{a\tau + b}{c\tau + d}.$$

This is the **Möbius action** of $\text{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ on the upper half-plane. Two values of $\tau$ describe the same lattice (hence the same curve, hence the same $j$) precisely when they are related by such a transformation:
$$j\!\left(\frac{a\tau + b}{c\tau + d}\right) = j(\tau) \quad \text{for all } \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \in \text{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}).$$

**Visualization: the upper half-plane and its fundamental domain.** Every $\text{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$-orbit in the upper half-plane has a unique representative in the region
$$\mathcal{F} = \left\{\tau = x + iy : |x| \leq \tfrac{1}{2}, \ y > 0, \ |\tau| \geq 1\right\},$$
called the **fundamental domain**. Picture:
```
             Im
              ^
              |
   .          |          .            <-- lattices with |τ|=1
    \         |         /                are on the arc
     \        |        /
      \       *i       /
       \______|______/               <-- vertical arc |τ|=1
       |      |      |
       |  F   |  F   |                <-- vertical strips
       |      |      |                    |Re τ| ≤ 1/2
       |______|______|
       |             |
       |             |
   ----+------+------+-----> Re
     -1/2   0     1/2
```
Every lattice shape is represented by exactly one $\tau \in \mathcal{F}$, so $\mathcal{F}$ parameterizes the space of elliptic curves over $\mathbb{C}$. The $j$-invariant becomes a bijection $j: \mathcal{F} \to \mathbb{C}$, giving each shape a numerical name.

Two lattices in the fundamental domain are the same iff they are literally the same $\tau$; this is why $j$ separates isomorphism classes.

### Fundamental domain and reduced quadratic forms

Suppose we want to classify lattices whose $\tau$ satisfies a specific integer quadratic equation
$$A\tau^2 + B\tau + C = 0, \qquad B^2 - 4AC = -D < 0.$$
Such a $\tau$ is the parameter of a lattice with an *extra* endomorphism (see § 15B: these are exactly the lattices with complex multiplication by the order of discriminant $-D$). The quadratic form $(A, B, C)$ determines $\tau$ uniquely via the quadratic formula, and a change of basis of the lattice corresponds to an $\text{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ change of the quadratic form.

Under a basis change, the quadratic form $(A, B, C)$ transforms to a new form $(A', B', C')$ with the *same* discriminant $-D$. Two forms with the same discriminant correspond to the same lattice-with-endomorphism if and only if they lie in the same $\text{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ orbit. The number of orbits is the **class number** $h(-D)$.

**Placing $\tau$ in the fundamental domain gives a canonical reduced form.** The conditions $|\text{Re}\,\tau| \leq \tfrac{1}{2}$ and $|\tau|^2 \geq 1$ translate directly into conditions on the coefficients:

From $\tau = (-B + i\sqrt{D})/(2A)$: $\text{Re}(\tau) = -B/(2A)$. The bound $|\text{Re}(\tau)| \leq 1/2$ gives $|B| \leq A$.

Compute $|\tau|^2 = \text{Re}(\tau)^2 + \text{Im}(\tau)^2 = B^2/(4A^2) + D/(4A^2) = (B^2 + D)/(4A^2)$. Since $D = 4AC - B^2$, this simplifies to $|\tau|^2 = C/A$. The bound $|\tau|^2 \geq 1$ gives $A \leq C$.

Combined: **the reduced quadratic form** satisfies $|B| \leq A \leq C$. Every $\text{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$-orbit contains exactly one reduced form.

**Finiteness: the search for reduced forms terminates.** From $D = 4AC - B^2 \geq 4A^2 - A^2 = 3A^2$ (using $C \geq A$ and $B^2 \leq A^2$), we get $A \leq \sqrt{D/3}$. So the enumeration of reduced forms of discriminant $-D$ is a triple loop:
1. $A = 1, 2, \ldots, \lfloor \sqrt{D/3} \rfloor$;
2. For each $A$, $B = -A, -A+1, \ldots, A$;
3. For each $(A, B)$, $C = (B^2 + D)/(4A)$, kept only if it's a positive integer with $C \geq A$.

Each valid triple is one class. For $D = 3$: $A = 1$, $B = -1, 0, 1$; only $B = -1$ gives $C = 1$ (integer) with $C \geq A$; and $B = 1$ gives $C = 1$ too, but is equivalent to $B = -1$ up to sign. Result: $h(-3) = 1$, one class. This class corresponds to the CM by the Eisenstein integers $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ (§ 15B) — the type of CM secp256k1 has.

The class number $h(-D)$ is the number of curves (up to isomorphism over $\mathbb{C}$) with CM by the order of discriminant $-D$. The **Hilbert class polynomial** $H_D(x) = \prod_{[A,B,C]} (x - j(\tau_{A,B,C}))$ has these $j$-values as its roots and is a polynomial with *integer* coefficients — a fact whose deep meaning is that the CM curves live over algebraic number fields, and their $j$-invariants are algebraic integers. This is the entry point to the analytic theory of CM curves used in Elkies' explicit isogeny computation and in constructing pairing-friendly curves.

## § 15B. Complex multiplication and the GLV method

Every elliptic curve has the endomorphisms $[n]$ for $n \in \mathbb{Z}$ (multiplication by an integer). A **CM curve** has *more*: extra endomorphisms whose action isn't just repeated addition. These extras come from special algebraic structure in the curve equation — an absent term that opens up a substitution symmetry. Concretely, only two families of curves over prime fields carry practical CM: those with equation $y^2 = x^3 + b$ (CM by $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$, $j = 0$) and those with equation $y^2 = x^3 + ax$ (CM by $\mathbb{Z}[i]$, $j = 1728$). The first family contains secp256k1 ($y^2 = x^3 + 7$).

The practical payoff is the **Gallant-Lambert-Vanstone (GLV) method**: on a curve with CM, the extra endomorphism $\psi$ is essentially free to compute, and scalar multiplication $[k]P$ can be split into two smaller multiplications, cutting the running time in half.

### Why generic curves have $\text{End}(E) = \mathbb{Z}$

Over a random elliptic curve $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$ (both $a, b$ nonzero and unrelated), the only endomorphisms are the integer multiplications $[n]$. Reason: an endomorphism $\phi(x, y) = (R_1(x), y R_2(x))$ (§ 8) that isn't of the form $[n]$ would require the curve equation to admit a nontrivial substitution symmetry — a substitution $x \to \alpha x + \beta$, $y \to \gamma y + \delta$ that preserves the equation. For the generic short Weierstrass form, no such substitution exists other than the trivial identity and the negation $y \to -y$ (which gives $[-1]$).

Specific structural constraints on the curve equation are what enable extra endomorphisms:
- The equation $y^2 = x^3 + b$ has *no* $x^2$ or $x$ term, only $x^3$ and a constant. This is the constraint that enables $\psi(x, y) = (\beta x, y)$ (see below).
- The equation $y^2 = x^3 + ax$ has no $x^2$ or constant term, only $x^3$ and a linear-in-$x$. This enables $\psi(x, y) = (-x, iy)$.

Both families are measure-zero within the moduli space of elliptic curves — random $(a, b)$ don't land in them. The corresponding $j$-invariants are $j = 0$ for $y^2 = x^3 + b$ (plugging $a = 0$ into the $j$ formula) and $j = 1728$ for $y^2 = x^3 + ax$ (plugging $b = 0$). Curves with any other $j$-invariant — Curve25519, P-256, and almost all curves used in practice — have $\text{End}(E) = \mathbb{Z}$ over the base field.

### CM by $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$: the $y^2 = x^3 + b$ family

Consider the curve
$$y^2 = x^3 + b$$
over $\mathbb{F}_p$ (secp256k1 is the case $b = 7$). Try the substitution $x \to \beta x$, $y \to y$ for a scalar $\beta$:
$$y^2 = (\beta x)^3 + b = \beta^3 x^3 + b.$$
For the equation to be preserved, we need $\beta^3 = 1$. If the equation had a nonzero $x^2$ or linear-$x$ term, the substitution would produce $\beta^2 x^2$ or $\beta x$ on the right side, which the left side (still $y^2$) can't match — the map would fail. The absence of those terms is the structural vacancy that lets the map exist.

For this to be usable over $\mathbb{F}_p$, a nontrivial cube root of unity $\beta \in \mathbb{F}_p$ must exist. Cube roots of unity are roots of $\beta^3 - 1 = (\beta - 1)(\beta^2 + \beta + 1)$; the root $\beta = 1$ is trivial, and the other two roots are roots of $\beta^2 + \beta + 1 = 0$, which has solutions in $\mathbb{F}_p$ iff its discriminant $-3$ is a square mod $p$, iff $p \equiv 1 \pmod 3$. For secp256k1's prime $p = 2^{256} - 2^{32} - 977$, direct check shows $p \equiv 1 \pmod 3$, so a nontrivial $\beta$ exists.

**Why $\psi(x, y) = (\beta x, y)$ respects the group law.** The map preserves the curve equation, so it sends points on $E$ to points on $E$; but we need more — the map must respect the chord-and-tangent construction, $\psi(P_1 + P_2) = \psi(P_1) + \psi(P_2)$.

Given $P_1 = (x_1, y_1)$ and $P_2 = (x_2, y_2)$ with $x_1 \neq x_2$, the group law says $P_1 + P_2 = P_3$ where $x_3 = \lambda^2 - x_1 - x_2$ and $\lambda = (y_2 - y_1)/(x_2 - x_1)$. Now the slope through $\psi(P_1) = (\beta x_1, y_1)$ and $\psi(P_2) = (\beta x_2, y_2)$ is
$$\lambda' = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{\beta x_2 - \beta x_1} = \beta^{-1} \lambda.$$
The new $x$-coordinate is
$$x_3' = (\lambda')^2 - \beta x_1 - \beta x_2 = \beta^{-2} \lambda^2 - \beta(x_1 + x_2).$$
Using $\beta^3 = 1$, so $\beta^{-2} = \beta$:
$$x_3' = \beta \lambda^2 - \beta(x_1 + x_2) = \beta(\lambda^2 - x_1 - x_2) = \beta x_3.$$
So $x_3'$ matches the $x$-coordinate of $\psi(P_3) = (\beta x_3, y_3)$, and the same check works for $y_3'$. So $\psi$ commutes with the chord-and-tangent construction: it is an endomorphism.

**The polynomial $\psi$ satisfies.** Apply $\psi$ repeatedly:
$$\psi^3(x, y) = (\beta^3 x, y) = (x, y),$$
so $\psi^3 = [1]$ in $\text{End}(E)$. Factor: $\psi^3 - [1] = (\psi - [1])(\psi^2 + \psi + [1]) = 0$. Since $\beta \neq 1$, $\psi \neq [1]$, so the second factor must vanish:
$$\psi^2 + \psi + [1] = 0 \qquad \text{in } \text{End}(E).$$

This is *exactly* the polynomial that the primitive cube root of unity $\omega = e^{2\pi i / 3}$ satisfies in $\mathbb{C}$: $\omega^3 = 1$, $\omega \neq 1$, so $\omega^2 + \omega + 1 = 0$. Algebraically, $\psi$ and $\omega$ satisfy the same relation, so the ring $\mathbb{Z}[\psi] \subset \text{End}(E)$ is isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}[\omega] \subset \mathbb{C}$ — the **Eisenstein integers**. This is what "complex multiplication by $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$" means: the endomorphism ring contains a copy of the Eisenstein integers.

### CM by $\mathbb{Z}[i]$: the $y^2 = x^3 + ax$ family

For the curve $y^2 = x^3 + ax$ (no constant term, no $x^2$), try $x \to -x$, $y \to iy$ where $i^2 = -1$:
$$(iy)^2 = (-x)^3 + a(-x) \implies -y^2 = -x^3 - ax \implies y^2 = x^3 + ax. \checkmark$$
The equation is preserved. Both $x^3$ and $ax$ pick up minus signs (odd powers of $x$), the $y^2$ picks up $i^2 = -1$, and the signs balance. A constant term $+b$ would not flip and would break the substitution — as would an $x^2$ term, which is even in $x$.

For $\psi(x, y) = (-x, iy)$ to be usable over $\mathbb{F}_p$, we need $i \in \mathbb{F}_p$, which exists iff $-1$ is a square mod $p$, iff $p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$.

**The polynomial $\psi$ satisfies.** Iterate: $\psi^2(x, y) = \psi(-x, iy) = (x, i^2 y) = (x, -y)$. But $(x, -y) = -P$ in the group law, so $\psi^2 = [-1]$. Rearranging:
$$\psi^2 + [1] = 0.$$
This is $i^2 + 1 = 0$: $\psi$ satisfies the same polynomial as the imaginary unit $i \in \mathbb{C}$. The ring $\mathbb{Z}[\psi] \subset \text{End}(E)$ is isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ — the **Gaussian integers**.

Curves with CM by $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ have $j = 1728$. They appear in some pairing-friendly constructions (variants of the Barreto-Naehrig family) but are less common in production than the $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ CM. The two production ecosystems where $j = 0$ CM matters are secp256k1 (Bitcoin, Ethereum) and BLS12-381 (Ethereum consensus).

### The scalar $\lambda$ and the GLV decomposition

Take secp256k1 with its cube-root-of-unity endomorphism $\psi$. Points on the curve inside the prime-order subgroup have order exactly $r$ (the "$n$" in Bitcoin's parameter set, a 256-bit prime). Since endomorphisms preserve the group structure, $\psi$ maps the prime-order subgroup into itself.

On a prime-order subgroup, every endomorphism acts as scalar multiplication. Why: pick a generator $G$; then $\psi(G) \in \langle G \rangle$, so $\psi(G) = [\lambda] G$ for some $\lambda \in \mathbb{Z}/r\mathbb{Z}$. For any point $P = [k] G$ in the subgroup, $\psi(P) = \psi([k] G) = [k] \psi(G) = [k\lambda] G = [\lambda]([k] G) = [\lambda] P$. So
$$\psi(P) = [\lambda] P \quad \text{for all } P \text{ in the prime-order subgroup.}$$

**Finding $\lambda$ algebraically.** Since $\psi$ satisfies $\psi^2 + \psi + [1] = 0$ in $\text{End}(E)$, and $\psi$ acts as $[\lambda]$ on the subgroup, $[\lambda]^2 + [\lambda] + [1] = [\lambda^2 + \lambda + 1]$ acts as zero on the subgroup. But the only scalar that kills the whole subgroup is a multiple of $r$, so
$$\lambda^2 + \lambda + 1 \equiv 0 \pmod{r}.$$
Solving by the quadratic formula: $\lambda \equiv (-1 \pm \sqrt{-3})/2 \pmod r$. For this to have a solution, $-3$ must be a square mod $r$; secp256k1's $r$ satisfies $r \equiv 1 \pmod 3$, which guarantees $\left(\frac{-3}{r}\right) = 1$ by quadratic reciprocity. Two solutions exist: $\lambda$ and $\lambda^2 \pmod r$ (they are the two nontrivial cube roots of unity mod $r$). Which of the two you pick is a convention; libsecp256k1 hard-codes a specific value.

**Why scalars live in $\mathbb{Z}/r\mathbb{Z}$.** For any $P$ of order $r$, scalar multiplication depends only on the scalar mod $r$: $[k + r]P = [k]P + [r]P = [k]P + \mathcal{O} = [k]P$. So $[k]$ and $[k + r]$ act identically on the subgroup, and the natural quotient identifying them is $\mathbb{Z}/r\mathbb{Z}$.

### The GLV decomposition itself

To compute $[k]P$ for a 256-bit scalar $k$ on secp256k1, the standard method takes 256 sequential doublings and (on average) 128 additions — about $O(\log k)$ point operations.

Using $\psi$, decompose $k$ as
$$k \equiv k_1 + k_2 \lambda \pmod r$$
with $k_1, k_2$ both roughly 128 bits (half the size of $k$). Then
$$[k]P = [k_1] P + [k_2 \lambda] P = [k_1] P + [k_2] \psi(P).$$
The point $\psi(P)$ costs one field multiplication to compute (just $x \to \beta x$; the $y$-coordinate is unchanged). The two half-length scalar multiplications $[k_1] P$ and $[k_2] \psi(P)$ can be interleaved with **Straus-Shamir simultaneous multiplication**, doing 128 doublings total (not 256) and mixing in additions of the appropriate combination of $P$ and $\psi(P)$ at each step.

Net saving: half the doublings for the same point count. On secp256k1, this is roughly a $30$–$40\%$ speedup for scalar multiplication, which translates directly into faster ECDSA signing and verification. Bitcoin's libsecp256k1 uses this optimization; Ethereum's precompiles do too.

**Computing $(k_1, k_2)$ from $k$.** The decomposition is done via a fixed 2D lattice reduction. The lattice $L = \{(a, b) \in \mathbb{Z}^2 : a + b\lambda \equiv 0 \pmod r\}$ has determinant $r$, and a short basis $\{(a_1, b_1), (a_2, b_2)\}$ is computed once (via the extended Euclidean algorithm on $r$ and $\lambda$, followed by shortest-vector search — for a 2D lattice this is Gauss reduction, cheap). For secp256k1, libsecp256k1 hard-codes these basis vectors:
$$
\begin{aligned}
(a_1, b_1) &= (\texttt{0x3086d221a7d46bcde86c90e49284eb15},\; -\texttt{0xe4437ed6010e88286f547fa90abfe4c3}), \\
(a_2, b_2) &\text{ : the second reduced basis vector, similarly hard-coded.}
\end{aligned}
$$
Given a target scalar $k$, the decomposition is $k_1 = k - c_1 a_1 - c_2 a_2$ and $k_2 = -c_1 b_1 - c_2 b_2$, where $c_1 = \lfloor k b_2 / r \rceil$ and $c_2 = -\lfloor k b_1 / r \rceil$ (rounded to nearest integer). The rounding step takes constant time, so the overall decomposition is constant-time and safe against timing side channels.

**A sanity requirement: $\gcd(3, r) = 1$.** For the endomorphism ring's characteristic polynomial $\psi^2 + \psi + [1] = 0$ to have distinct roots mod $r$ (so that the scalar $\lambda$ is well-defined), the discriminant $-3$ must be a unit in $\mathbb{Z}/r$, i.e., $\gcd(3, r) = 1$. For secp256k1's $r$ (a specific 256-bit prime), $r$ is not $3$, so $\gcd(3, r) = 1$. This condition also guarantees that the prime-order subgroup contains no $3$-torsion elements from the "wrong" root of the polynomial, so $\psi$ maps the subgroup cleanly back into itself.

### Beyond GLV: GLS on curves without CM

For curves without base-field CM (Curve25519, P-256), a similar trick uses the Frobenius endomorphism $\pi$ acting on the curve over an extension field. This is the **GLS method** (Galbraith-Lin-Scott), which requires working in $\mathbb{F}_{p^2}$ rather than $\mathbb{F}_p$. It applies to curves like the twisted Edwards form of Curve25519, and the speedup is roughly comparable to GLV. In practice, most production ECC libraries use GLV where available (secp256k1) and stick to plain scalar multiplication elsewhere (Ed25519's high-degree extension optimizations are more specialized).

## § 16. Curve families and forms

Different curve forms exist because they have different **computational advantages** — they're not different mathematical objects, just different coordinate systems for the same underlying group.

### Short Weierstrass

$$y^2 = x^3 + ax + b.$$

The "default" form. Used by NIST curves (P-256 = secp256r1, P-384, P-521), Bitcoin's secp256k1.

**Pros**: Simplest equation. Easy to verify a point is on the curve.

**Cons**: Addition formula has corner cases ($P + P$, $P + (-P)$, $P + \mathcal{O}$) that complicate constant-time implementations. The $y$-coordinate is needed for the addition law, so you can't do "x-only" arithmetic.

### Montgomery

$$B y^2 = x^3 + A x^2 + x.$$

**Pros**: Admits an "x-only" arithmetic — you can compute $x([n]P)$ from $x(P)$ without ever needing $y$. This enables the **Montgomery ladder**, an extremely efficient and naturally constant-time scalar multiplication algorithm. The ladder doubles and adds in parallel, with a uniform computational profile regardless of the scalar's bit pattern.

**Cons**: Not every elliptic curve has a Montgomery form. For an elliptic curve to be expressible as Montgomery, two conditions: the group order must be divisible by 4, and there must be a point of order 2 and a point of order 4.

**Example**: Curve25519, $y^2 = x^3 + 486662 x^2 + x$ over $\mathbb{F}_{2^{255} - 19}$. The constant 486662 is chosen so that the curve order is $8 \cdot (\text{huge prime})$ and the twist order is $4 \cdot (\text{huge prime})$, making it twist-secure.

### Twisted Edwards

$$a x^2 + y^2 = 1 + d x^2 y^2.$$

(When $a = 1$: just "Edwards form"; otherwise "twisted.")

**Pros**: The addition law is **complete** — a single formula that works for all input pairs, including doubling, adding inverses, and adding the identity. No corner cases. This is huge for constant-time implementations: you don't need branches in your scalar multiplication code. The formula:
$$(x_1, y_1) + (x_2, y_2) = \left( \frac{x_1 y_2 + y_1 x_2}{1 + d x_1 x_2 y_1 y_2}, \frac{y_1 y_2 - a x_1 x_2}{1 - d x_1 x_2 y_1 y_2} \right).$$

**Cons**: Not every curve has an Edwards form; same kind of restriction as Montgomery (need 4-torsion).

**Example**: Ed25519. The curve is $-x^2 + y^2 = 1 - \frac{121665}{121666} x^2 y^2$ over $\mathbb{F}_{2^{255} - 19}$ — birationally equivalent to Curve25519 (the same underlying group, just different coordinates).

### Why "birationally equivalent" means "the same curve"

The user mentioned the Edwards form is a quartic, not a cubic, and asked whether it's "really" an elliptic curve. Yes — because there is a *change of variables* (a birational map) given by rational functions that converts the Edwards equation to a Weierstrass equation. The map is invertible (away from finitely many bad points), so the two curves describe the same group.

The change of variables for Montgomery $\leftrightarrow$ short Weierstrass: starting from $By^2 = x^3 + Ax^2 + x$, substitute
$$x = u - \tfrac{A}{3}, \quad y = v / B^{1/2}$$
(the second substitution requires $B$ to be a square in the field, otherwise you get a twist instead of the original curve). The result is short Weierstrass form.

For Edwards $\leftrightarrow$ Montgomery: the map
$$(x, y)_{\text{Edw}} \leftrightarrow (u, v)_{\text{Mon}}, \quad u = \frac{1 + y}{1 - y}, \quad v = \frac{1 + y}{(1 - y) x}.$$

(You can verify these by substitution: assume one curve equation, substitute, and confirm you get the other.)

The point of the form change is purely computational. Mathematically, all three forms describe the same underlying group object — the same group law, the same torsion structure, the same isogenies, the same $j$-invariant.

### Other forms briefly

- **Jacobi quartic**: $y^2 = x^4 + ax^2 + 1$. Used historically; now superseded by Edwards.
- **Hessian form**: $x^3 + y^3 + 1 = 3 d x y$. Has unified addition law. Used in some research contexts.
- **Fermat cubic**: $x^3 + y^3 = 1$ (or $= d$). Is an elliptic curve, isomorphic to $y^2 = x^3 - 432 d^2$ via a rational change of variables. Mostly of historical/aesthetic interest.

### Specific named curves

| Curve | Form | Field | Used in |
|---|---|---|---|
| **secp256k1** | Short W: $y^2 = x^3 + 7$ | $\mathbb{F}_p$, $p = 2^{256} - 2^{32} - 977$ | Bitcoin, Ethereum |
| **secp256r1 / P-256** | Short W: $y^2 = x^3 - 3x + b$ | $\mathbb{F}_p$, $p = 2^{256} - 2^{224} + ...$ | TLS, NIST |
| **Curve25519** | Montgomery: $y^2 = x^3 + 486662 x^2 + x$ | $\mathbb{F}_p$, $p = 2^{255} - 19$ | X25519 (Diffie–Hellman) |
| **Ed25519** | Twisted Edwards: $-x^2 + y^2 = 1 - \tfrac{121665}{121666} x^2 y^2$ | $\mathbb{F}_p$, $p = 2^{255} - 19$ | EdDSA signatures |
| **Curve448** | Untwisted Edwards: $x^2 + y^2 = 1 - 39081 x^2 y^2$ | $\mathbb{F}_p$, $p = 2^{448} - 2^{224} - 1$ | TLS 1.3 (higher security) |
| **P-384, P-521** | Short Weierstrass | $\mathbb{F}_p$ for various $p$ | TLS, NIST |
| **BLS12-381** | Short Weierstrass over a "pairing-friendly" prime | $\mathbb{F}_p$ | Ethereum 2.0, zk-SNARKs |

Notes on the design choices:
- secp256k1's "$y^2 = x^3 + 7$" was chosen by Satoshi to be obviously unrigged — there are no parameters to hide a backdoor in. The "k" in the name historically stood for the Koblitz family that motivated curves like this.
- secp256r1's "$b$" was generated from a hash of a seed by NIST/NSA. The provenance of the seed is the controversial part.
- Curve25519 was chosen by Bernstein to be fast: the prime $2^{255} - 19$ is just below $2^{255}$, allowing efficient modular reduction; the constant 486662 was chosen to minimize attack surface while making the curve order $8 \times$ prime.
- BLS12-381 and similar **pairing-friendly curves** have a small embedding degree (12 in this case) deliberately, so that pairings are computable. They're used in zk-SNARK constructions and threshold signatures.

## § 17. Coordinate systems and the point at infinity

### Affine versus projective

So far we've described curve points as pairs $(x, y)$ with $y^2 = f(x)$. These are **affine coordinates**: they describe points in the ordinary plane.

The problem: the "point at infinity" $\mathcal{O}$ has no affine coordinates. We added it as a separate special case.

The fix: switch to **projective (homogeneous) coordinates**, where every point in the plane gets a triple $(X : Y : Z)$ with the convention that $(X : Y : Z)$ and $(\lambda X : \lambda Y : \lambda Z)$ represent the *same* point for any nonzero $\lambda$. We recover affine coordinates by $x = X/Z$, $y = Y/Z$, which makes sense as long as $Z \neq 0$.

What about $Z = 0$? In affine coordinates, $Z = 0$ corresponds to $x = X/0$ and $y = Y/0$, which "blow up to infinity." So the locus $Z = 0$ in projective space *is* the set of points at infinity. For each "direction" $(X : Y : 0)$ there's one point at infinity.

### The point at infinity on an elliptic curve

The curve $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$ in projective coordinates becomes
$$Y^2 Z = X^3 + a X Z^2 + b Z^3,$$
obtained by substituting $x = X/Z$, $y = Y/Z$ and clearing denominators.

Setting $Z = 0$: $0 = X^3$, so $X = 0$. Then $Y$ is free, so we have $(0 : Y : 0) = (0 : 1 : 0)$ (after normalizing). **This is the point at infinity**: a single point with projective coordinates $(0 : 1 : 0)$.

So the affine "extra point" $\mathcal{O}$ is just the projective point $(0 : 1 : 0)$ — perfectly ordinary in projective coordinates, no special-casing required.

### Connection to computer vision

Computer vision uses homogeneous coordinates for exactly the reason elliptic curves do: they let you handle "infinity" consistently without case-splits. Parallel lines in the world meet at a "vanishing point" in the image, which in projective coordinates is just a regular point with $Z = 0$.

The mathematical structure is identical. In computer vision:
- World point $(X, Y, Z)$ in 3D.
- Image point $(x, y, 1)$ after perspective projection, with $x = X/Z$, $y = Y/Z$.
- A point on the horizon (a "vanishing point") corresponds to $Z = 0$, where the projection blows up.

In elliptic curves:
- Affine point $(x, y)$.
- Projective embedding $(X : Y : Z)$ with $x = X/Z$, $y = Y/Z$.
- Point at infinity = $Z = 0$.

In both cases, "infinity" is not a separate exception — it's just where $Z$ vanishes. The point at infinity on an elliptic curve and the vanishing point in a picture are the same mathematical phenomenon: a regular projective point that *looks* infinite when you try to express it in affine coordinates.

### Jacobian coordinates for efficiency

In Jacobian coordinates, the scaling is more aggressive: $(X : Y : Z)$ corresponds to affine point $x = X/Z^2$, $y = Y/Z^3$. Why this weird convention?

Because in elliptic curve formulas, $x$ has "degree 2" and $y$ has "degree 3" — meaning the substitutions in § 15A (isomorphism-preserving coordinate changes) that preserve the curve form are $x \to u^2 x$, $y \to u^3 y$. Jacobian coordinates respect this scaling: $(X, Y, Z) \sim (u^2 X, u^3 Y, u Z)$.

The practical payoff: in Jacobian coordinates, the addition law has *no inversions* — only multiplications and squarings. Field inversion is expensive (a 256-bit modular inversion is roughly 100× slower than a multiplication), so eliminating it from the inner loop is a massive speedup. Standard ECC implementations use Jacobian coordinates internally and convert back to affine only at the end.

The doubling formula in Jacobian for $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$:
$$X_3 = (3 X^2 + a Z^4)^2 - 8 X Y^2,$$
$$Y_3 = (3 X^2 + a Z^4)(4 X Y^2 - X_3) - 8 Y^4,$$
$$Z_3 = 2 Y Z.$$

No divisions, all just multiplication and squaring of field elements.

### Point compression

A point $(x, y)$ on $y^2 = f(x)$ is essentially determined by $x$ alone, since $y$ can only be one of two values $\pm \sqrt{f(x)}$. So you can store half the data: just $x$, plus one bit indicating whether $y$ is "even" or "odd" (because $p$ is an odd prime $y$ and $p-y$ always have opposite parities).

To decompress: given $x$ and the parity bit, compute $y^2 = f(x)$, take a square root in $\mathbb{F}_p$ (this requires the Tonelli–Shanks algorithm or similar; cheap in certain cases:
 
- In the case of $p \equiv 3 \mod 4$ (e.g., NIST P-256, secp256k1), 
  $\sqrt{a} = a^{(p+1)/4}$ works directly, then pick the square root with the indicated parity.

- In the case of $p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ (e.g., Edwards25519 / Curve25519), 
  we look at its structure modulo 8. If $p \equiv 5 \pmod 8$, we can write it as $p = 8k + 5$.
  Curve25519 uses the prime $p = 2^{255} - 19$. $$\text{Since } 2^{255} \text{ is a multiple of 8 (for any power } \ge 3\text{): } 0 - 19 = -19 \equiv 5 \pmod 8$$
  we choose $b = a^{\frac{p+3}{8}}$, then; $a^{\frac{p+3}{4}} = a^{\frac{p-1+4}{4}} = a^{\frac{p-1}{4} + 1} = a^{\frac{p-1}{4}} \cdot a$ .
  the term $a^{\frac{p-1}{4}}$ must equal either $+1$ or $-1$ (since $\left(a^{\frac{p-1}{4}}\right)^2 = a^{\frac{p-1}{2}} = 1$).
	  If $a^{\frac{p-1}{4}} = 1$, $b$ is exactly the square root we wanted: $\sqrt{a} = b$.
	  If $a^{\frac{p-1}{4}} = -1$, $b^2 = -1 \cdot a = -a \pmod p$, To fix this and isolate $\sqrt{a}$, we multiply both sides by $\sqrt{-1}$: $$\sqrt{a} = b \cdot \sqrt{-1} \pmod p$$(a field contains a square root of $-1$ if and only if $p \equiv 1 \pmod 4$)

### Square-root algorithms in finite fields

The two general-purpose algorithms for computing square roots in $\mathbb{F}_p$ when the fast-case formulas don't apply.

**Tonelli–Shanks Algorithm:** Given a quadratic residue $a$ modulo $p$, it finds $\sqrt a$ by using the fact that $a^{(p-1)/2}=1$. It decomposes $p-1=2^s q$ to separate out the powers of 2 in the group order, since $x$ and $-x$ have the same square, so the ambiguity in taking square roots is entirely tied to these powers of 2; by isolating this $2^s$ part, once we know that $a^q$ lies in the subgroup of order $2^s$ (so $(a^q)^{2^s}=1$), we repeatedly square $a^q$ to find the smallest $m$ such that $(a^q)^{2^m}=1$ ($s$ is the maximum possible exponent). If $m=0$ we are done, otherwise we multiply by a carefully chosen element whose order is also a power of 2 (coming from a quadratic non-residue) to reduce this exponent. Iterating this process decreases $m$ step by step until we reach an element whose square is $a$.

**Cipolla's Algorithm:** 
Assume $p$ is an odd prime and $a$ is a quadratic residue modulo $p$, so there exists some $x \in \mathbb{F}_p$ satisfying $x^2 = a$. Cipolla’s algorithm does not try to find $x$ directly inside $\mathbb{F}_p$. Instead, it chooses some $u \in \mathbb{F}_p$ for which $d = u^2 - a$ is a quadratic **non-residue** modulo $p$.
Since $d$ has no square root in $\mathbb{F}_p$, adjoining a formal element $\sqrt{d}$ creates a genuinely larger field:
$$\mathbb{F}_{p^2} = \{x + y\sqrt{d} : x, y \in \mathbb{F}_p\}, \qquad (\sqrt{d})^2 = d.$$
The crucial fact is $(\sqrt{d})^p = -\sqrt{d}$ (the elements fixed by $x \mapsto x^p$ are precisely the elements of $\mathbb{F}_p$, but $\sqrt{d} \notin \mathbb{F}_p$).
Now define $\alpha = u + \sqrt{d}.$
Then $\alpha^p = (u + \sqrt{d})^p = u - \sqrt{d}.$
- $\alpha^{p+1} = \alpha^p \alpha = (u - \sqrt{d})(u + \sqrt{d}) = u^2 - d = u^2 - (u^2 - a) = a.$
- $r = \alpha^{(p+1)/2}$
- $r^2 = \alpha^{p+1} = a.$
$r$ is a square root of $a$. Moreover, $r$ actually lies back in $\mathbb{F}_p$, not merely in $\mathbb{F}_{p^2}$. Since $r^2 = a$, we have
$$r^p = \frac{r^{p+1}}{r} = \frac{a}{r} = r,$$
and the elements fixed by $x \mapsto x^p$ are precisely the elements of $\mathbb{F}_p$.
So the algorithm is:
1. Pick $u$ until $u^2 - a$ is a quadratic non-residue.
2. Compute $\left(u + \sqrt{u^2 - a}\right)^{(p+1)/2}$.

## § 18. Practical attacks and defenses

### The invalid curve attack on NIST curves

The NIST short Weierstrass formulas
$$\lambda = \frac{3x^2 + a}{2y}, \quad x_{\text{new}} = \lambda^2 - 2x, \quad y_{\text{new}} = \lambda(x - x_{\text{new}}) - y$$
use only $a$ from the curve equation, not $b$. So if the implementation accepts $(x, y)$ without first checking that $(x, y)$ lies on the system's curve $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b_{\text{system}}$, the attacker can supply a point on a *different curve* $y^2 = x^3 + ax + b'$ for any $b'$ they like — they just pick any $(x', y')$ and back-solve $b' = y'^2 - x'^3 - ax'$.

The attacker can choose $b'$ to make the resulting curve have a small subgroup. Doing scalar multiplication $kP$ in that small subgroup leaks $k \mod (\text{small order})$, which the attacker can recover. Repeating with different small subgroups gives $k$ via CRT.

**Defense**: explicit point validation. Before any scalar multiplication, check that the incoming point satisfies the *system's* curve equation, not just any equation. One line of code, but missing it is catastrophic.

### Why X25519 is immune

X25519 only transmits the $x$-coordinate (not $y$). The Montgomery ladder operates only on $x$-coordinates. Given an attacker-supplied $x$:
- If $f(x) = x^3 + 486662 x^2 + x$ is a square in $\mathbb{F}_p$, the point $(x, \pm\sqrt{f(x)})$ is on the curve.
- If $f(x)$ is a non-square, the point is on the **twist**, $-y^2 = f(x)$.

There is no "third option," because the attacker has no $y$ to fudge — the Montgomery equation pins down the curve once $x$ is fixed. So the implementation always operates either on the curve or on its twist, both of which are twist-secure for Curve25519.

The twist attack is the only thing left to worry about, and Curve25519 was designed to defeat it: both the curve order ($8 \cdot \ell$, $\ell$ prime) and the twist order ($4 \cdot \ell'$, $\ell'$ prime) have only the small cofactors 8 and 4. The "discrete log in a subgroup of order 4" or "of order 8" leaks only 2–3 bits of the key, which is then cleared by the cofactor multiplication at the end of the key derivation. Attack defeated.

- **Small subgroup attack**: forcing the protocol to operate in a small subgroup. Defended by clearing the cofactor (e.g., multiplying every input by 8 in Curve25519).

## § 19. Schnorr signatures and Ed25519

### The Schnorr construction

The standard Schnorr signature:
1. Setup: a group $G$ with generator $G$ of large prime order $\ell$. Private key $x \in \mathbb{Z}/\ell$, public key $A = xG$.
2. Sign a message $m$:
   - Pick a random nonce $r \in \mathbb{Z}/\ell$. Compute $R = rG$.
   - Compute challenge $e = H(R \,\|\, A \,\|\, m)$ where $H$ is a hash function.
   - Compute response $s = r + e x \mod \ell$.
   - Signature is $(R, s)$.
3. Verify: check that $sG \stackrel{?}{=} R + eA$.

**Why this works**: the verification equation expands to
$$s G = (r + ex) G = rG + (ex) G = R + e \cdot (xG) = R + eA. \checkmark$$

**Why it's secure** (sketch, not a full proof): the challenge $e$ is unpredictable because it depends on $R$ (which is fresh per signature) and on $m$. An attacker who could forge would essentially need to find $s$ such that $sG = R + eA$ for an arbitrary $R$ they don't know the discrete log of — equivalent to solving discrete log in $G$.

### Ed25519 specifics

Ed25519 implements Schnorr signatures on Curve25519 (in Edwards form), with two specific design choices:

**Deterministic nonces**: instead of generating $r$ randomly, Ed25519 derives it from $r = H(H(\text{seckey}) \,\|\, m)$. This eliminates the disastrous "bad RNG" failure mode that plagued ECDSA (where a repeated nonce leaks the private key, as seen in the Sony PS3 hack).

**Including the public key in the challenge**: $e = H(R \,\|\, A \,\|\, m)$ includes $A$, defending against "related-key" attacks where the attacker controls one of several public keys related by known relations.

**Cofactor 8**: Curve25519 has cofactor 8, so the verification equation in Ed25519 is actually $8 s G \stackrel{?}{=} 8 R + 8 e A$ — multiplying both sides by 8 to clear the small subgroup. This is a security/usability tradeoff: it makes signatures slightly slower to verify but immune to small-subgroup confusion.

| Schnorr concept | Ed25519 implementation |
|---|---|
| Commitment $R$ | $R = rG$ where $r = H(H(\text{seckey}) \,\|\, m)$ — deterministic |
| Challenge $e$ | $e = H(R \,\|\, A \,\|\, m)$ — binds public key |
| Response $s$ | $s = r + e \cdot \text{seckey} \mod \ell$ |
| Verification | $8 s G \stackrel{?}{=} 8 R + 8 e A$ — cofactor-cleared |

## § 21. EC in real protocols: TLS, SSH, signatures

The previous sections describe the math; here is where it actually shows up in the wild, what the choices were, and what they imply for detection engineering and protocol analysis.

### TLS 1.3 key exchange

TLS 1.3 (RFC 8446) supports a small whitelist of curves for the (EC)DHE handshake. The `key_share` extension carries the client's curve selection and ephemeral public key. In practice, the universe is:

| `NamedGroup` | Curve | Public key size | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| `x25519` (0x001D) | Curve25519 (Montgomery) | 32 bytes | Default for nearly all modern clients (Chrome, Firefox, OpenSSL ≥ 1.1.1) |
| `x448` (0x001E) | Curve448 (Edwards) | 56 bytes | ~224-bit security, used when 128 isn't enough |
| `secp256r1` (0x0017) | P-256 | 65 bytes uncompressed (33 compressed) | NIST/FIPS-mandated; common in regulated environments and older clients |
| `secp384r1` (0x0018) | P-384 | 97/49 bytes | NIST, used at 192-bit security level |
| `secp521r1` (0x0019) | P-521 | 133/67 bytes | Rarely seen in TLS, more in S/MIME, JOSE |

The X25519 public key is always 32 bytes, transmitted as the little-endian $u$-coordinate (just the Montgomery $x$-coordinate). NIST curves transmit `0x04 || x || y` (uncompressed) by default in TLS 1.3, though `0x02/0x03 || x` (compressed) is also valid.

**Why X25519 became default.** Three reasons, in descending order of weight: (1) it's faster than the NIST curves on most CPUs (single-scalar Montgomery ladder vs. complete-addition Weierstrass with field inversions); (2) it's constant-time by construction (the ladder doesn't branch on key bits); (3) it has no curve parameters that anyone might have backdoored — the curve constants are derived from $2^{255} - 19$ via a publicly defensible procedure. Curve25519's design predates TLS 1.3 by several years (Bernstein, 2005), but TLS 1.2 didn't have a slot for it until late and treated it as exotic; TLS 1.3 made it first-class.

### TLS signature algorithms

Beyond key exchange, TLS uses ECC for certificate signatures and CertificateVerify messages. The slate:

- **ECDSA with P-256, P-384, P-521** (`ecdsa_secp256r1_sha256`, etc.) — the default for ECDSA certificates issued by public CAs.
- **Ed25519** (`ed25519`) — used in some certificate ecosystems (Let's Encrypt issues Ed25519, though uptake is partial; SSH and Tor use it heavily).
- **Ed448** (`ed448`) — rare, mostly for high-security regulated use.

ECDSA dominates TLS certificates by volume because of CA/Browser Forum policy inertia (the first standardized EC signature was ECDSA; Ed25519 came later and isn't universal). For *fresh* deployments, Ed25519 is preferred where supported because of (1) deterministic nonces (no bad-RNG catastrophes — see Sony PS3 ECDSA failure), (2) cleaner addition law, (3) faster verification.

### SSH host keys and user keys

OpenSSH supports the following key types in modern versions:

| Key type | Algorithm | Curve / params | When used |
|---|---|---|---|
| `ssh-ed25519` | EdDSA | Ed25519 | Recommended default since OpenSSH 6.5 (2014) |
| `ssh-rsa` / `rsa-sha2-512` | RSA-PKCS#1 | 3072+ bits | Legacy, still widespread |
| `ecdsa-sha2-nistp256/384/521` | ECDSA | NIST P-256/384/521 | Available since OpenSSH 5.7, less common than Ed25519 |
| `sk-ssh-ed25519@openssh.com` | EdDSA + FIDO/U2F | Ed25519 + security key | Hardware-backed signing |
| `sk-ecdsa-sha2-nistp256@openssh.com` | ECDSA + FIDO/U2F | P-256 + security key | Same, NIST curve flavor |

The `sk-` variants invoke a YubiKey/Titan/etc. — the host key on the server side is regular Ed25519/ECDSA, but the signing happens inside the security key. The wire format wraps the signature with a `flags` byte (for user-presence/user-verification) and a `counter` (for replay detection).

### ECDSA in detail (still dominant, worth understanding)

For curve $E$ with base point $G$ of order $n$, private key $d \in \mathbb{Z}/n$, public key $Q = dG$. To sign message $m$ with hash $h = H(m)$ (taken as integer mod $n$):

1. Pick a per-signature nonce $k \in \mathbb{Z}/n$ (uniformly at random, or deterministically per RFC 6979).
2. Compute $R = kG$ and $r = R_x \mod n$.
3. Compute $s = k^{-1}(h + r d) \mod n$.
4. Output $(r, s)$.

Verify: compute $u_1 = h s^{-1}$, $u_2 = r s^{-1}$, then $R' = u_1 G + u_2 Q$. Accept iff $R'_x \equiv r \mod n$.

**Why ECDSA over Schnorr historically?** Patent reasons. Schnorr's signature scheme was patented (US 4,995,082, expired 2008); ECDSA was deliberately designed in the early 1990s to be Schnorr-equivalent in security but algebraically different enough to dodge the patent. Once the patent expired, Schnorr/EdDSA became preferable, but ECDSA was already entrenched.

**The famous ECDSA pitfalls:**

1. **Repeated nonces**: if you sign two different messages with the same $k$, anyone can recover $d$. From $s_1 = k^{-1}(h_1 + rd)$ and $s_2 = k^{-1}(h_2 + rd)$, subtract: $s_1 - s_2 = k^{-1}(h_1 - h_2)$, so $k = (h_1 - h_2)/(s_1 - s_2)$, then $d = (s_1 k - h_1)/r$. This is the **PS3 hack** (Sony reused $k$ across firmware signatures), and it has hit Bitcoin wallets multiple times.

2. **Biased nonces**: if $k$ is taken from a biased distribution (e.g., only 250 bits instead of 256), lattice attacks (Howgrave-Graham–Smart, Bleichenbacher, the **"Minerva" and "TPM-FAIL" attacks**) can recover $d$ from a few thousand signatures. Constant-time implementations are essential.

3. **Malleability**: if $(r, s)$ is a valid signature, so is $(r, -s \mod n)$. Bitcoin's BIP-0062 mandates low-$s$ canonicalization to prevent transaction-ID malleability attacks.

4. **Curve-specific weaknesses**: small subgroup, invalid curve, twist attacks — all discussed in § 18.

### What to look for as a defender (detection-engineering perspective)

For TLS handshakes:
- **Client Hello supported_groups extension**: see the curve list the client offers. A handshake offering only `secp256r1` is likely older/regulated; one with `x25519` first is modern.
- **Server's selected curve in key_share**: reflects server policy.
- **JA3/JA4 fingerprints**: include the supported_groups list — useful for client identification.
- **Curve mismatches/downgrades**: a connection where supported_groups omits X25519 but the server picks P-256 anyway is normal; a connection where the client offered X25519 and server chose P-192 (deprecated, RFC 8447 forbids) is a misconfiguration or attack indicator.

For SSH:
- **Host key fingerprints in known_hosts**: Ed25519 fingerprints are 256 bits; ECDSA P-256 keys produce 256-bit fingerprints too, distinguishable by the `ssh-ed25519` vs `ecdsa-sha2-nistp256` algorithm name in the line.
- **Algorithm negotiation (KEXINIT)**: clients/servers list supported algorithms; the chosen `kex_algorithms` (e.g., `curve25519-sha256@libssh.org`) and `host_key_algorithms` tell you what's in use.
- **Authorized_keys**: a sudden change to a key with `sk-ssh-ed25519@openssh.com` indicates the user moved to hardware-backed auth (or an attacker is impersonating that).

## § 22. Isogeny-based post-quantum cryptography

Standard ECDH and ECDSA are broken by Shor's algorithm on a sufficiently large quantum computer — the discrete log on $E(\mathbb{F}_q)$ falls to the same algorithm that factors RSA. Post-quantum replacements come from problems that *don't* reduce to abelian group discrete log. Isogeny-based crypto is one family of these: the underlying hard problem is to **find an isogeny between two given elliptic curves**, given that you know the curves but not the path between them.

The setting: fix a "large" prime $p$ and consider all supersingular elliptic curves over $\mathbb{F}_{p^2}$. There are roughly $p/12$ such curves (up to isomorphism). For each prime $\ell \neq p$, each supersingular curve has exactly $\ell + 1$ isogenies of degree $\ell$ to other supersingular curves. Joining these into a graph (vertices = curves, edges = $\ell$-isogenies) gives a highly connected "isogeny graph" with rich structure.

The hard problem: given two vertices in this graph, find a path between them. With current best classical algorithms, this takes $O(\sqrt{p})$ time (similar to discrete log via baby-step-giant-step). With Shor, no known speedup — quantum doesn't help because the action of the isogeny graph isn't a clean abelian group.

### SIDH and the 2022 break

**SIDH** (Supersingular Isogeny Diffie-Hellman, Jao–De Feo 2011) was the marquee scheme. The protocol:

- Alice picks a secret isogeny $\phi_A: E_0 \to E_A$ of degree $2^a$.
- Bob picks a secret isogeny $\phi_B: E_0 \to E_B$ of degree $3^b$.
- They publish $(E_A, \phi_A(P_B), \phi_A(Q_B))$ and $(E_B, \phi_B(P_A), \phi_B(Q_A))$ where $P, Q$ are public auxiliary torsion points.
- The shared secret is the $j$-invariant of $E_0 / \langle \ker \phi_A, \ker \phi_B \rangle$, which both parties can compute by combining their secret with the other's public auxiliary points.

**SIKE** (the NIST submission based on SIDH) reached Round 4 of the NIST PQC standardization. Then, in July 2022, Castryck and Decru published a polynomial-time classical attack — followed within weeks by improvements from Maino-Martindale and Robert. The attack exploits the auxiliary torsion-point information: those auxiliary points carry enough algebraic structure that, given $E_A$ and the torsion-point images, you can use a 2-dimensional **Kani's lemma** glueing construction to recover $\phi_A$ in polynomial time. SIKE was retracted.

The deeper lesson: the SIDH protocol leaked too much. It published not just the target curve but also the *images of torsion points* on it — and that ancillary information turned out to be cryptographically catastrophic. Schemes that hide the torsion-point images are still considered viable.

### CSIDH (still standing)

**CSIDH** (Castryck–Lange–Martindale–Panny–Renes 2018, "C-side" pronounced "see-side") uses a different structure: the action of an *abelian group* (the ideal class group of a CM order) on the set of supersingular curves over $\mathbb{F}_p$ (not $\mathbb{F}_{p^2}$). This restricted setting has commutative group action: $\mathfrak{a} * (\mathfrak{b} * E) = (\mathfrak{a} \mathfrak{b}) * E = \mathfrak{b} * (\mathfrak{a} * E)$. Diffie-Hellman in this group action gives a key exchange where both parties walk through the isogeny graph and arrive at the same curve regardless of order.

CSIDH parameters target 128-bit classical security at $p \approx 512$ bits, with key sizes about 64 bytes and key exchange in ~40 ms on typical hardware. Quantum security is more debated — Kuperberg's subexponential quantum algorithm for abelian hidden shift applies, requiring larger parameters for safety against future quantum attackers.

### SQIsign (signatures from isogenies)

**SQIsign** (De Feo–Kohel–Leroux–Petit–Wesolowski 2020) is a signature scheme: prove knowledge of a secret isogeny $\phi_{\rm sk}: E_0 \to E_{\rm pk}$ without revealing it. The signature is itself an isogeny, but with a clever structure that proves "I could compute this" without leaking $\phi_{\rm sk}$.

Signature sizes are small (~200 bytes) compared to other PQ signatures (Dilithium: 2420 bytes; SPHINCS+: 8 KB), making SQIsign attractive for size-constrained protocols. The downside: signing is slow (seconds, not milliseconds) because it requires computing isogenies of large degree. NIST is evaluating SQIsign in the additional-signatures round (results expected 2026-2027).

### What the dual isogeny does in all of this

The whole isogeny-graph framework runs on duals. Every edge $\phi: E \to E'$ in the graph has a dual edge $\hat\phi: E' \to E$. Walking forward and backward through the graph means composing forward isogenies with dual isogenies. Vélu's formulas give you forward isogenies cheaply (given a kernel subgroup, output the isogeny and the target curve). Duals let you compose without going through expensive recomputation. The security proofs for SIDH (before the attack), CSIDH, and SQIsign all rely on the dual to express "you know an isogeny iff you know its dual."

## § 23. Pairing-based cryptography: BLS signatures and beyond

Pairings enable cryptographic constructions that don't exist (or are clumsy) without them. Three classes:

### BLS signatures (Boneh–Lynn–Shacham, 2001)

The construction:
- Public group $G$ of prime order $r$, with a pairing $e: G \times G \to G_T$ where $G_T$ is the target group (a multiplicative subgroup of some $\mathbb{F}_{q^k}^*$).
- A generator $g$ and a hash function $H: \{0,1\}^* \to G$.
- Private key $\text{sk} \in \mathbb{Z}/r$, public key $\text{pk} = g^{\text{sk}}$.
- Sign $m$: output $\sigma = H(m)^{\text{sk}}$. **Single group element.** No nonce.
- Verify $(m, \sigma)$: check $e(\sigma, g) \stackrel{?}{=} e(H(m), \text{pk})$.

The verification works because by bilinearity, $e(\sigma, g) = e(H(m)^{\text{sk}}, g) = e(H(m), g)^{\text{sk}} = e(H(m), g^{\text{sk}}) = e(H(m), \text{pk})$. The two sides are equal iff the signer knew $\text{sk}$.

**Properties that make BLS popular:**

- **Deterministic**: no nonce required, no PS3-style RNG failures possible.
- **Aggregation**: $n$ signatures $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_n$ on messages $m_1, \ldots, m_n$ from $n$ public keys $\text{pk}_1, \ldots, \text{pk}_n$ aggregate to $\sigma_{\rm agg} = \prod_i \sigma_i$ — a single group element. Verification of the aggregate: $e(\sigma_{\rm agg}, g) \stackrel{?}{=} \prod_i e(H(m_i), \text{pk}_i)$.
- **Compact**: a signature is one group element (~48 bytes for BLS12-381's compressed form).

**Where used:**

- **Ethereum 2.0 (Beacon Chain)**: validators sign attestations with BLS on BLS12-381. ~500,000 active validators, all aggregable. The aggregate signature on each "epoch" is one ~48-byte element instead of 500,000 separate signatures.
- **Chia blockchain**: BLS aggregation for transactions.
- **Filecoin**: BLS aggregation for storage proofs.
- **Drand** (distributed randomness beacon): threshold BLS for beacon outputs.

**BLS12-381 specifically**: $p$ is a 381-bit prime chosen so that the embedding degree is 12 (large enough for security: Weil-pairing target $\mathbb{F}_{p^{12}}^*$ has order $p^{12} - 1$, which for 381-bit $p$ gives a ~4500-bit target group — discrete-log-secure to ~128 bits). The curve is $y^2 = x^3 + 4$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$, twisted variant over $\mathbb{F}_{p^2}$. **BLS12-381** is the de-facto standard pairing-friendly curve since ~2017.

### Identity-based encryption (Boneh–Franklin, 2001)

The first scheme to make IBE practical. Setup: a trusted authority (PKG, "private key generator") publishes master public key $\text{mpk} = g^s$ for secret $s$. Anyone can encrypt to identity $\text{id}$ (an email address, say) using only $\text{mpk}$ and $\text{id}$ — no per-recipient certificate or key fetch. Decryption requires the PKG to issue a private key $\text{sk}_{\rm id} = H(\text{id})^s$ to the holder of that identity.

The cleverness: $e(\text{sk}_{\rm id}, g) = e(H(\text{id})^s, g) = e(H(\text{id}), g^s) = e(H(\text{id}), \text{mpk})$ — the recipient and the encryptor compute the same shared value through different paths, enabling a hybrid encryption scheme.

Where used: limited adoption (Voltage Security, now part of Micro Focus, deployed it for email encryption). The KGC's master secret is a single-point-of-trust issue that has kept IBE niche, but it's a clean illustration of what pairings enable that's impossible without them.

### Zero-knowledge proofs (KZG commitments, SNARKs)

The **Kate–Zaverucha–Goldberg polynomial commitment** scheme commits to a polynomial $f(X) \in \mathbb{F}_r[X]$ as a single group element $C = g^{f(\tau)}$ where $\tau$ is a secret from a trusted setup. To prove "$f(z) = y$," the prover sends $\pi = g^{(f(X) - y)/(X - z) \text{ evaluated at } \tau}$ — a single group element. The verifier checks $e(C - g^y, h) \stackrel{?}{=} e(\pi, h^\tau - h^z)$ — one pairing equation.

KZG is the engine of:

- **PLONK**, **Marlin**, and most modern SNARK systems.
- **Ethereum's EIP-4844 (proto-danksharding)**: blob commitments are KZG commitments on BLS12-381. This went live on Ethereum mainnet in March 2024 with the Dencun upgrade. Blob data is committed via KZG, and rollups (Optimism, Arbitrum, etc.) post their compressed transactions as blobs.

So pairings, originally an exotic mathematical curiosity, now sit in the hot path of Ethereum block production. Anyone monitoring Ethereum network traffic in 2024+ sees pairing computations happening constantly.

### The MOV attack (the other side of the coin)

The Menezes–Okamoto–Vanstone attack uses the Weil pairing to **reduce ECDLP to multiplicative-group DLP**. Given $P, Q = nP$ on $E$, pick any $R \in E[n]$ of order matching $n$. Compute $e_n(P, R)$ and $e_n(Q, R) = e_n(P, R)^n$ — both elements of $\mu_n \subset \mathbb{F}_{q^k}^*$. Now the problem $Q = nP$ has become $e_n(Q, R) = e_n(P, R)^n$ in $\mathbb{F}_{q^k}^*$, where sub-exponential index-calculus methods can solve discrete log.

**When this is dangerous**: when $k$ (the embedding degree) is small enough that $\mathbb{F}_{q^k}$ is computationally accessible. For $k = 6$ and a 256-bit curve, the target group has $\sim 1500$ bits — well within reach of number-field-sieve methods. The Menezes–Okamoto–Vanstone result killed naive use of supersingular curves (which always have $k \leq 6$).

**For pairing-friendly curves** (BLS12-381, BN254): $k$ is chosen large enough that $\mathbb{F}_{q^k}^*$ DLP is *as hard as* curve DLP. So BLS12-381's $k = 12$ gives target field of ~4500 bits ≈ 128-bit DLP security. The curve and target are matched. For *non-pairing-friendly* curves (Curve25519, P-256, secp256k1), $k$ is exponentially large, MOV is impractical, and pairings aren't computable anyway.

## § 24. Discrete log algorithms

A defender or analyst needs to know how attacks on ECDLP actually work. The list:

### Generic (no curve structure exploited)

- **Pollard rho**: random walk with cycle detection. $O(\sqrt{n})$ time, $O(1)$ space. The state of the art for "ordinary" elliptic curves. For Curve25519 (group order $\approx 2^{252}$), this is $2^{126}$ operations — infeasible.
- **Baby-step giant-step**: $O(\sqrt{n})$ time, $O(\sqrt{n})$ space. Less used than Pollard rho because of memory.
- **Pohlig-Hellman**: reduce DLP to DLP in each prime-order subgroup. If $n = \prod \ell_i^{e_i}$, the cost is $\sum \sqrt{\ell_i^{e_i}}$ — dominated by the largest prime factor of $n$. This is why curve orders are chosen to have a *large prime factor*; if $n$ factors entirely into small primes, the curve is broken.

### Structure-exploiting (curve-specific)

- **MOV attack** (above): transfers ECDLP to multiplicative-group DLP via pairings. Applies when embedding degree is small.
- **Anomalous curves attack** (Semaev–Smart–Satoh–Araki): if $\#E(\mathbb{F}_p) = p$ exactly, the discrete log can be computed in *polynomial time* via a "lift" to $\mathbb{Q}_p$. So curves where $t = 1$ are catastrophically broken. (Easily detected: avoid curves with $\#E = p$.)
- **Index-calculus for high-genus curves**: doesn't apply to elliptic curves (genus 1), but applies to higher-genus hyperelliptic curves and makes their DLP weaker than elliptic.
- **GHS attack**: for curves over $\mathbb{F}_{2^n}$ with composite $n$, can transfer ECDLP to a smaller-genus curve's DLP. Avoidable by choosing prime $n$ or non-Weil-restriction-vulnerable parameters.

### Quantum

- **Shor's algorithm**: solves ECDLP in polynomial time on a fault-tolerant quantum computer. Logical qubit requirements: ~2300 qubits for a 256-bit curve with ~$10^{10}$ T-gates — well beyond current hardware (~1000 noisy qubits as of 2026), but a target for transitioning to PQ before it becomes feasible.

### What this implies for curve parameter choice

A curve $E/\mathbb{F}_p$ is safe iff:
1. $\#E$ has a large prime factor (≥ 250 bits for 128-bit security).
2. Embedding degree $k$ is large enough that $\mathbb{F}_{p^k}^*$ DLP is at least as hard as ECDLP (typically $k \geq 100$ for non-pairing-friendly curves).
3. $\#E \neq p$ (not anomalous).
4. $\#E \neq p \pm 1$ (avoid trace 0, ±1 edge cases that sometimes weaken specific subgroups).
5. Field characteristic large prime (avoid GHS-vulnerable composite extension degree).
6. **Twist secure**: the quadratic twist also has order with large prime factor.

All standardized curves (Curve25519, P-256, secp256k1, BLS12-381) satisfy these. Curves of opportunity (the kind an adversary might foist via invalid-curve attacks) typically don't, which is why point validation is essential.

## § 25. Threshold signatures and multi-party EC protocols

The single-signer model — one private key, one signer — is increasingly inadequate. Real systems want:

- **Custody**: no single hardware module holds the full private key.
- **Quorums**: $t$-out-of-$n$ signers approve a transaction.
- **Privacy**: signers shouldn't reveal which of them participated.

Elliptic curves enable threshold and multi-party signature schemes with single-signature output size (one group element + one scalar, same as a regular signature).

### FROST (Flexible Round-Optimized Schnorr Threshold)

For Schnorr-style signatures (Ed25519, secp256k1 Schnorr):

1. **Distributed key generation (DKG)**: parties run a protocol that produces shares $d_i$ of the private key $d = \sum_i d_i$ (or, more precisely, Lagrange-coefficient-weighted shares from a polynomial secret sharing). Public key $Q = dG$ is publicly known.
2. **Signing round 1 (commitment)**: each participating signer $i$ picks two nonces $a_i, b_i$ and broadcasts commitments $(D_i, E_i) = (a_i G, b_i G)$.
3. **Signing round 2 (response)**: parties agree on a binding factor $\rho_i = H(i, m, \{D_j, E_j\})$, compute commitment $R_i = D_i + \rho_i E_i$, aggregate $R = \sum R_i$, compute challenge $c = H(R, Q, m)$, and each party outputs $z_i = (a_i + \rho_i b_i) + c \cdot \lambda_i d_i$ where $\lambda_i$ is the Lagrange coefficient.
4. **Combine**: $z = \sum z_i$, output $(R, z)$ as the Schnorr signature.

The output $(R, z)$ is indistinguishable from a single-signer Schnorr signature. Verifiers see no quorum metadata. Used in:

- **Coinbase, MPCH (Multi-Party Custody Hub), and most institutional custody platforms** for Bitcoin/Ethereum.
- **DAO governance** for some protocols.
- **Nostr** (decentralized social) for threshold-signed key recovery.

### MuSig2

Specifically for Bitcoin Taproot. $n$-of-$n$ multi-signature aggregation in two rounds. After Taproot activation (November 2021), all signers in a "tapscript" can collaboratively produce a single Schnorr signature that looks identical to a single-signer signature on-chain, hiding the multi-party structure from outside observers.

### Threshold ECDSA (harder)

ECDSA's $k^{-1}$ inversion makes threshold ECDSA significantly harder than threshold Schnorr — you can't just sum partial signatures. Modern protocols (GG18/GG20 by Gennaro-Goldfeder, CMP21 by Canetti et al., MTA by Lindell-Nof) use Paillier homomorphic encryption or oblivious transfer to handle the inversion. Round complexities of 3-7 rounds, with significant communication per signing.

Used in:
- **Fireblocks, Coinbase Custody, Anchorage** for Bitcoin/Ethereum institutional custody.
- **ZenGo wallet** for consumer custody.

### Distributed key generation pitfalls

DKG protocols are subtle. Vulnerabilities have included:
- **Biased keys** (a malicious participant can influence the output public key, making it weaker).
- **Rogue-key attacks** (an attacker registers a public key derived from existing public keys, then claims to have "always known" the secret). MuSig defends against this by hashing each party's public key.
- **Subliminal channels** (a malicious signer can leak information through their commitment choice).

For detection: changes to key-share metadata in custody systems, unusual co-signing patterns, or signers participating from new locations are operational signals worth monitoring even though the signatures themselves look ordinary on-chain.

## § 26. Side-channel and implementation attacks

For a curve to be cryptographically broken in practice, the math doesn't have to break — the implementation has to leak.

### Constant-time arithmetic

A scalar multiplication $[k]P$ that branches on bits of $k$ leaks the entire scalar through timing or power. The defenses:

- **Montgomery ladder** (X25519): processes bits uniformly, doubling and adding at every step regardless of bit value. Constant-time by construction.
- **Complete addition formulas** (Edwards form): no branching for "$P = Q$" vs "$P \neq Q$" vs "$P = -Q$" — the formula works in all cases.
- **Window methods with table lookups**: must access all table entries (not just the indexed one) to prevent cache-timing side channels.

### Specific historical attacks

- **TPM-FAIL (2019)**: bias in nonce generation on Intel SGX and STMicroelectronics TPMs allowed ECDSA private-key recovery in seconds with as few as 35 signatures.
- **Minerva (2020)**: similar bias in Athena IDProtect smart cards. Key recovery from ~1000 signatures.
- **Yubikey Mini PKCS#11 attack (EUCLEAK, 2024)**: side-channel allows ECDSA key extraction from older Yubikey 5 series via electromagnetic emanations during signing.
- **CacheBleed / Spectre-style attacks** on OpenSSL ECDSA scalar multiplication: cache-timing side channels in the per-signature nonce path.

### Fault-injection attacks

- **Bellcore attack** on RSA-CRT (generalizable to ECDSA): single bit flip during signature computation leaks key.
- **Skip-instruction attacks**: skip the point-validation step (§ 18) by glitching the CPU during the check.

### Practical implications

For a defender or detection engineer:
- Pure software ECDSA on a server is generally fine if the library is well-audited (OpenSSL post-1.1.1, BoringSSL, libsodium).
- Smart cards and hardware tokens have a long history of side-channel issues. New FIPS 140-3 certifications mitigate some, but not all.
- Detection of side-channel exploitation: usually unobservable in normal logs; detection happens via key-rotation incidents, anomaly detection on signature timing/counts, or post-hoc forensic analysis.

## § 27. Curves in practice: what you'll actually encounter

A practical lookup table for which curves you'll see in different ecosystems:

| Use case | Dominant curve | Why |
|---|---|---|
| TLS 1.3 KEX (most modern clients) | X25519 | Fast, twist-secure, no NIST optics |
| TLS 1.3 KEX (regulated/FIPS) | secp256r1 | NIST-mandated for FIPS 140-3 |
| TLS certificate signing | ECDSA-P256 dominant; Ed25519 growing | CA ecosystem inertia |
| SSH host/user keys | Ed25519 default since ~2018 | OpenSSH chose it |
| Bitcoin | secp256k1 (ECDSA + Schnorr) | Satoshi's choice; Schnorr added in Taproot |
| Ethereum (txs) | secp256k1 (ECDSA) | EVM compatibility |
| Ethereum 2.0 (validator consensus) | BLS12-381 (BLS signatures) | Aggregation for scalability |
| Tor | Curve25519 (ntor handshake), Ed25519 (relay identity) | Performance + safety |
| Signal / Wire / Matrix (E2EE) | Curve25519 (X3DH) | Performance, designed-in |
| Zcash (sapling) | Jubjub (on BLS12-381) | zk-friendly inside SNARKs |
| Filecoin / Drand | BLS12-381 | Aggregation |
| ZRTP (encrypted VoIP) | Curve25519 | Performance |
| WireGuard | Curve25519 | Performance, no nonces needed |
| FIDO2/WebAuthn (passkeys) | ECDSA-P256 (default), Ed25519 (alg=-8 EdDSA) | Standard requires both; P-256 more universal |
| EMV (chip-and-PIN cards) | NIST P-256 mostly, some Brainpool | Banking industry conservatism |
| Bluetooth pairing (LE Secure Connections) | NIST P-256 | Spec mandate |

A sample of "weird" curves you might encounter and what they signal:
- **Brainpool curves** (`brainpoolP256r1`, etc.): EU-designed alternatives to NIST. Some German/EU government systems, some PGP keys.
- **secp192r1, secp224r1**: legacy NIST. Avoid in new deployments; 96/112-bit security.
- **Curve41417**: rare; appears in some research/CSIDH parameter selections.
- **Pallas/Vesta** (Halo2 SNARK system): non-pairing-friendly cycle of curves used for recursive proofs in Mina protocol and Zcash Orchard.
