From two values of y to a torus and a period lattice

The same object is viewed in four ways. Above an ordinary x-value there are two opposite y-values. At four special x-values those choices meet. Joining the two sheets at all four places creates a surface with one handle; its two non-shrinkable loops then produce the two period directions in the u-plane.

1

An ordinary x-value lifts to two curve points

Over the complex numbers, the two choices are y and −y; “upper” and “lower” are only visual names for the two sheets
y² = x³ + ax + b
f(x) = x³ + ax + b
Fix x = x₀. The equation becomes y² = f(x₀). When f(x₀) ≠ 0, its two solutions are opposite:
y₀ and −y₀, where y₀² = f(x₀).
Keeping the two choices separate gives two local copies of the x-sphere. Moving around a special x-value swaps which copy the solution belongs to, so the copies must meet there.
The meeting values are e₁, e₂, e₃, and ∞. At each finite root eᵢ, f(eᵢ) = 0, so y = −y = 0. Infinity supplies the fourth meeting value for a cubic.
first sheet second sheet values where the sheets meet
The two black points over x₀ are distinct. At each red x-value they coincide, so those are the places where a path can pass from one sheet to the other.
2

Two cross-gluings create one handle

The four meeting values are paired into two cuts; the second connection is what leaves a hole
A

Pair the four meeting values

Draw two disjoint cuts on the x-sphere: one from e₁ to e₂, and one from e₃ to ∞. Their endpoints are exactly the four places where the sheets meet.

B

Open both sheets along both cuts

Make the same two openings in each copy. Every opening now has two banks, one on each side of the cut.

C

Glue each opening to the other sheet

Cross-gluing the first cut connects the sheets once. Cross-gluing the second cut connects them again by a separate route. The gap between those routes cannot be filled.

D

Round the same surface into a torus

No further cutting or gluing occurs. Bending and smoothing the two-connection surface turns the gap into the visible central hole of a torus.

2
The second join supplies the handleIt connects the same two sheets by another route, leaving a closed passage between the two routes. That passage is the torus hole.
3

The handle has two independent loop directions

Both loops are drawn from the same torus coordinates, so the paths sit on the surface rather than floating beside it
γ₁

Runs around the tube. It closes after circling the short direction once.

γ₂

Runs around the central hole. It closes after circling the long direction once.

Solid segments face the viewer; dashed segments lie on the back of the torus. Each curve is generated from the same projected torus surface, which keeps the path and its hidden part aligned.
Integrate the same differential along each loop
dx / y
Going once around a closed loop returns to the same curve point, but the integral may have accumulated a nonzero change.
change accumulated around γ₁
ω₁ = ∮γ₁ dx / y
change accumulated around γ₂
ω₂ = ∮γ₂ dx / y
Why exactly two period directions? Any closed route on a torus can wind some whole number of times around γ₁ and some whole number of times around γ₂. The integral therefore changes by mω₁ + nω₂.
4

The two loop integrals become the two lattice vectors

All positions are computed from one origin and the same two vectors, so translated points and labels remain aligned
u(P) = ∫P₀P dx / y
Choose a starting point P₀. Two paths from P₀ to the same endpoint P can differ by closed loops. Adding γ₁ changes u by ω₁; adding γ₂ changes it by ω₂.
Λ = ℤω₁ + ℤω₂
Thus u, u + ω₁, u + ω₂, and every u + mω₁ + nω₂ describe the same point P on the curve.
The shaded parallelogram is one complete copy of the torus. Every other cell is a translated duplicate. Identifying corresponding points in all cells gives ℂ / Λ.
The vectors are not parallel: they encode the two independent loop directions of the handle. A single line of repeats would describe only one periodic direction, not a torus.
Blue edges differ by ω₁. Identifying the left and right edge closes one torus direction.
Orange edges differ by ω₂. Identifying the lower and upper edge closes the other direction.
The four black points are placed by exact vector addition from the same base point u. The dashed arrows therefore end precisely at u + ω₁ and u + ω₂ rather than being positioned by eye.
