Modular group

The fundamental domain of SL2(ℤ) on the upper half-plane

Each lattice shape in \(\mathbb{C}\) is determined, up to rescaling, by the period ratio \(\tau = \omega_2/\omega_1 \in \mathbb{H}\). Different bases of the same lattice give different \(\tau\)'s, related by the transformation \(\tau \mapsto (a\tau+b)/(c\tau+d)\) with \(\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \in \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})\). The shaded region \(\mathcal{F}\) picks exactly one \(\tau\) from each equivalence class.

Fundamental domain

\(\mathcal{F} = \Big\{ \tau \in \mathbb{H} : |\mathrm{Re}\,\tau| \le \tfrac{1}{2},\ |\tau| \ge 1 \Big\}\)
every \(\tau \in \mathbb{H}\) has exactly one representative in \(\mathcal{F}\)

Upper half-plane, tiled by translates of \(\mathcal{F}\)

SL2(ℤ) · \(\mathcal{F}\) covers \(\mathbb{H}\)

The solid blue region is \(\mathcal{F}\). The lighter dashed regions are its neighbors under the two generators of \(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})\): the translation \(T: \tau \mapsto \tau + 1\) (adjacent vertical strips) and the inversion \(S: \tau \mapsto -1/\tau\) (the semicircular region inside \(|\tau| = 1\)). Iterated \(T\)'s and \(S\)'s tile the entire upper half-plane by copies of \(\mathcal{F}\). Two example points outside \(\mathcal{F}\) are shown together with the transformation carrying each of them back into \(\mathcal{F}\).
1Two boundary conditions

Every \(\tau \in \mathbb{H}\) can be moved into the vertical strip \(|\mathrm{Re}\,\tau| \le \tfrac{1}{2}\) by subtracting an integer (an iterate of \(T\)), and can be moved outside the unit disc \(|\tau| \ge 1\) by applying \(S\) if it sat inside. Alternating these two moves terminates: each application of \(S\) inside the disc strictly increases the imaginary part.

2Special points

\(\tau = i\): the square lattice \(\mathbb{Z}[i]\), fixed by \(S\).
\(\tau = \rho = e^{2\pi i / 3}\) at the left corner, and \(-\bar\rho = e^{\pi i / 3}\) at the right corner: the hexagonal lattice \(\mathbb{Z}[\rho]\), fixed by \(ST\). These are the only \(\tau \in \mathcal{F}\) with nontrivial \(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})\)-stabilizer, so the corresponding elliptic curves have extra automorphisms.

3The \(j\)-invariant

The \(j\)-invariant is a bijection \(j: \mathcal{F} \to \mathbb{C}\). It assigns to each lattice-shape a complex number so that \(j(\tau) = j(\tau') \iff\) the two lattices give isomorphic elliptic curves. Well-known values: \(j(i) = 1728\) and \(j(\rho) = 0\).
