Fenchel–Young equality

The conjugate is the downward shift that creates the supporting line

Fix a slope \(y\). Start with the line \(t\mapsto yt\). The conjugate gives exactly the amount that must be subtracted from that line so it lies below \(f\) and touches it.

measure the largest excess of \(yt\) above \(f\)
\[ f^{*}(y)=\sup_{u\in\mathbb{R}}\bigl(yu-f(u)\bigr) \]
At the maximizing point \(t^{*}\), this is the vertical distance from \(f(t^{*})\) up to \(yt^{*}\).
subtract this same number from the whole line
the shifted line is the highest one that stays below \(f\)
\[ \ell_y(t)=yt-f^{*}(y)\leq f(t) \]
The shift makes \(\ell_y(t^{*})=f(t^{*})\); raising the line any further would make it cross the graph.
conjugate of the absolute-value function
\[ f^{*}(y)= \begin{cases} 0, & |y|\leq 1,\\ +\infty, & |y|>1. \end{cases} \]
For every \(y\in[-1,1]\), the line \(yt\) already passes through the corner and stays below \(|t|\).
finite exactly for the slopes that support the corner
the derivative is replaced by an interval
\[ \partial f(0)=[-1,1] \]
Every slope in this interval gives equality in Fenchel–Young at \(x=0\).
