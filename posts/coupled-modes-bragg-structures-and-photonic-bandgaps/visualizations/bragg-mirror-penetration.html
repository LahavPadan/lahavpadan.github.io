From an imaginary Bloch wavenumber to a measurable mirror

A finite Bragg mirror is a two-boundary problem

The gap relation explains why a field can decay. It does not, by itself, determine how much is reflected by a grating of length L. That requires the coupled forward and backward envelopes together with the far-end condition: no backward wave is incident from the right.

The periodic modulation continuously converts the two envelopes into one another

Local evolution

Forward amplitude A and backward amplitude B

dA/dz = iκB
dB/dz = −iκA
differentiate once → A″ = κ2A,  B″ = κ2B

The positive sign in the second-order equation is the mathematical signature of hyperbolic growth/decay rather than oscillation. The two independent solutions are e+κz and e−κz.

3

“Penetration depth” names two related but different length readings

The distinction prevents the common factor-of-two ambiguity.

Amplitude Bragg length

For a semi-infinite mirror at exact Bragg tuning, the non-growing eigenmode is

|E(z)| ∝ e−κz = e−z/LB.

After one LB=1/κ, the field amplitude is 1/e of its surface value.

Intensity decay length

Power or intensity is quadratic in field amplitude:

I(z) ∝ e−2κz = e−z/(LB/2).

Its e-folding length is therefore LB/2, not LB.

Effective reflection position

The centroid of that intensity-like exponential is

∫z e−2κzdz / ∫e−2κzdz = 1/(2κ).

This gives the useful exact-Bragg, long-mirror estimate Lpen≈LB/2.

!

Amplitude is not power

At κL=2.65, tanh(κL)≈0.990 is the amplitude reflection coefficient, while R=tanh²(κL)≈0.980. Requiring R>99% gives κL>atanh(√0.99)=2.993 — essentially three Bragg lengths.

The missing logical chain

The hyperbola supplies the imaginary wavenumber and hence the local decay scale. The finite mirror’s reflectivity comes only after the forward/backward coupled equations are solved with both boundaries. That boundary-value problem is why exponentials combine into cosh and sinh, and why the final answer is R=tanh²(κL).
