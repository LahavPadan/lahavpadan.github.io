The two-wave system is a block of the full Fourier matrix

A

Why these two rows?

The Fourier component $E_n$ has wavenumber $q_n=k+nG_1$.

For first-order backscattering, choose $G_1=2k_B$ and place the reference wavenumber near $k_B$.

$q_0\approx +k_B \qquad\text{and}\qquad q_{-1}\approx -k_B$

The diagonal mismatch depends on $q_n^2$. Therefore the two opposite wavenumbers have the same free-wave value and become resonant together.

$D_{-1}=D_0=0 \quad\text{at exact Bragg}$

A single grating step changes the forward component near $+k_B$ into the backward component near $-k_B$.

B

Why not the other rows?

At exact Bragg, the neighboring diagonal entries are already separated from zero.

$D_{-2}=D_{+1}=8k_B^2$

These rows have a large diagonal mismatch before any approximation is made. They are therefore not part of the resonant block.

C

How are they suppressed?

Use the first discarded equation, the row $n=+1$:

$-C E_0+D_{+1}E_{+1}-C E_{+2}=0$

The next amplitude, $E_{+2}$, is another weak-coupling step away. At leading order,

$E_{+1} \approx \frac{C}{D_{+1}}E_0$

With $C\approx k_B^2\Delta\varepsilon/(2\bar{\varepsilon})$ and $D_{+1}\approx8k_B^2$,

$\frac{E_{+1}}{E_0} \approx \frac{C}{D_{+1}} \approx \frac{\Delta\varepsilon} {16\bar{\varepsilon}}$

A large diagonal mismatch forces the off-resonant amplitude to be smaller by the weak-modulation factor.
