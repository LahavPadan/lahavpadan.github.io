The geometry behind the coupling term

The middle spring measures separation, not position

Each mass has its own displacement. The coupling spring only responds to how those two displacements change the distance between its endpoints.

Derive the extension from the endpoint coordinates

The spring does not read either displacement separately. First compute its new endpoint separation, then subtract the separation it had at equilibrium.

Equilibrium length L0 = B0 − A0
Displaced endpoints A = A0 + xA,    B = B0 + xB
Current length L = B − A = (B0 + xB) − (A0 + xA)
Extension ΔL = L − L0 = [(B0 + xB) − (A0 + xA)] − (B0 − A0) = xB − xA The fixed attachment coordinates cancel; only the change in endpoint separation remains.

xA = −xB  ⇒  |ΔL| = 2|xA|The endpoints separate or approach one another twice as quickly as either mass moves. The middle spring supplies its strongest restoring action.

The same geometry now enters Newton's law

From spring extension to the coupled equations of motion

Each line follows from the previous one: Hooke's law for the middle spring, then the two outer springs, then mẍ = F.

1

Apply Hooke's law to the middle spring

FA(κ) = +κΔL = κ(xB − xA)
FB(κ) = −κΔL = −κ(xB − xA)

When ΔL > 0, the middle spring is stretched: it pulls A to the right and B to the left. The two forces are equal and opposite.

→
2

Add the restoring force from each outer spring

FA = −kAxA + κ(xB − xA)
FB = −kBxB − κ(xB − xA)

The outer spring attached to A contributes −kAxA; the outer spring attached to B contributes −kBxB.

→
3

Set total force equal to mass times acceleration

mẍA = −(kA + κ)xA + κxB
mẍB = κxA − (kB + κ)xB

Collecting the xA and xB terms produces the two equations used in the article.

This is exactly where the coupling appears: the equation for A contains xB, and the equation for B contains xA. Without the middle spring, κ = 0 and those cross-terms disappear.
